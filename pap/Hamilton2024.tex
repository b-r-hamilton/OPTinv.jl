\begin{keypoints}
\item We infer surface properties from benthic records, accounting for age model uncertainty, circulation lags, and paleotemperature equations
\item A high-latitude ($>50^{\circ}$N) North Atlantic MCA-LIA cooling of 0.41 $\pm$ 0.13 K from 1140-1880 CE is reconstructed 
\item The identified cooling is consistent with marine surface reconstructions and is greater than that of the Last Millennium Reanalysis
\end{keypoints}
\begin{abstract}
  Paleoceanographic reconstructions indicate a large-scale cooling in
  the last millennium, but significant disagreement on the spatial
  pattern and timing still exists. This disagreement is partially due
  to the inference of surface conditions from sparse, noisy, and
  near-surface marine fauna that can exhibit seasonal and habitat
  depth biases.  Here, we infer spatially-varying surface changes from
  benthic sediment core records by back-propagation through an ocean
  circulation model, improving upon prior methodologies that inferred
  regional surface evolution from subsurface records. We present a
  surface temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$
  history that fits a dataset of 11 high-resolution benthic
  $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ records within error and also
  supports previous independent estimates from Common Era marine
  surface records. Our methodology accounts for the opposing and
  covarying behavior of temperature and
  $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ on
  $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$.  Our estimate is
  characterized by a high-latitude ($>50^{\circ}$N) North Atlantic-centered Little Ice Age sea
  surface temperature (SST) cooling of 0.41 $\pm$ 0.13 K  (1 s.e.) cooling from
  1140-1880 CE, which is within error of planktonic sea surface
  temperature (SST) record compilations and exceeds the cooling exhibited in the Last Millennium Reanalysis.
  We improve the detection of long-term climate signals by using
  benthic sediment core records which record the properties of
  abyssal waters that are not subject to high-energy near-surface
  variability, instead of planktonic sediment core records which tend
  to have more noise.  These results are consistent with the global
  SST trends inferred from the Ocean2k planktonic SST compilation and
  corroborate robust Little Ice Age cooling in the North Atlantic.
\end{abstract}

\section*{Plain Language Summary}
Historical instrumental evidence and climate proxy records both suggest that there was a period of anomalously cool global mean temperature, sometime in the past 500 years, called the Little Ice Age (LIA). Past estimates of oceanic cooling have been obtained from chemical analyses on the shells of single-celled organisms, called foraminifera, that dwell in the surface layer of the ocean and are preserved in the sediment when they die. However, planktonic foraminiferal records only reflect oceanic conditions at a particular surface point. Here, we build on prior techniques to infer the oceanic conditions over a wide surface area by using chemical analyses on the shells of bottom-dwelling foraminifera. These foraminifera record climate signals with higher fidelity, as they record bottom water properties that are composed of waters sourced from large areas of the surface ocean. We use an ocean circulation model to relate waters at the sediment core sites to the surface, and then infer broad swaths of surface water characteristics based on bottom-dwelling foraminifera records. We find that LIA cooling in the high-latitude North Atlantic was similar to previous estimates of the global LIA cooling from surface-dwelling foraminifera, but considerably larger than suggested by estimates driven with numerical models.

\section{Introduction}
The past 2,000 years, referred to as the Common Era (CE), is a
critical period for understanding the response of the Earth's climate
to various perturbations on timescales longer than that of the
instrumental record. One frequently observed characteristic of the
climate evolution of the CE is the Little Ice Age (LIA), a global
cooling anomaly that occurred in the past millennium and had
anomalously cool sea surface temperatures \cite <e.g.,>{ocean2k} as well as
surface air temperatures
\cite <e.g.,>{Consortium--2013:Continental}. Understanding the spatiotemporal
extent of the LIA is a key step in constraining the response of the
Earth's climate system to the known natural perturbations, which
include changing solar irradiance due to the precession of the
equinoxes, variable solar activity \cite <e.g.,>{lapointe2021}, and
volcanic stratospheric sulfate injection \cite <e.g.,>{Buntgen-Arseneault-2020:Prominent}.

Inferring the evolution of the LIA from observations is challenging
 due to the sparse and noisy nature of paleoceanographic
data. Specifically, LIA sea surface temperature
(SST) cooling rates have been inferred largely from planktonic
foraminifera \cite{ocean2k} that represent a single surface point and can be difficult to
extrapolate to a regional average. In addition, planktonic records are
subject to seasonal and habitat biases that can distort the recorded
signal. Both of these factors may contribute to conflicting values in
the magnitude and timing of the Medieval Climate Anomaly (MCA) to LIA
cooling. For instance \citeA[]{Cunningham-Austin-2013:Reconstruction}
estimated 0.2 $^{\circ}$C/kyr from 1200-1940 CE in the northeast North
Atlantic, while \citeA{ocean2k} estimated 0.42 $^{\circ}$C/kyr from
800-1800 CE globally.

In this paper, we develop an inverse approach to infer surface
properties from a compilation of benthic
$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ records. Previously, these
records were used to demonstrate that the
deep ocean MCA-LIA transition, as indicated by benthic records,
occurred at 1350 $\pm$ 50 CE, while the surface ocean MCA-LIA
transition, as indicated by planktonic records, occurred over a longer
time interval, ranging from 1100-1700 CE
\cite[]{Lu-Oppo-2023:Surface}. In addition, a larger magnitude LIA was
observed in the benthic records than in the planktonic records,
suggested to indicate an Arctic amplification of LIA temperature
changes, transferred to the sediment core sites by an active Atlantic
Meridional Overturning Circulation. Here, we quantitatively simulate
the effect of ocean circulation on core site signals, which allows us
to estimate surface conditions from benthic records.

We infer surface conditions from this dataset via back-propagation
through a modern-day circulation model under the assumption that there
were no large-scale circulation changes over the CE. We apply the empirical
ocean circulation model of
\citeA[]{Gebbie-Huybers-2012:mean} that was used to estimate coherent
regional temperature histories over the CE from a compilation of
marine surface records, observations from the 1870s HMS Challenger
expedition, and modern hydrography \cite{gebbie19}. Both the North
Atlantic and Antarctic surface regions had an amplified MCA-LIA
cooling relative to the global average, which was suggested to be due
to a winter-amplified cooling during the LIA and the sensitivity of
deepwater formation to winter conditions. In this work, we seek to refine the estimate of North
Atlantic surface temperature through the usage of benthic records
which may reflect more stable climatic signals than the planktonic
data used by \citeA{gebbie19}.

% JG: in future revisions, explain more about the motivation for this paragraph
We present a new methodology to invert for the time-evolving magnitude
of surface spatial modes as obtained through singular value
decomposition. These modes emphasize regions of deep water formation
and smoothly vary in space. This approach is similar to
\citeA{Amrhein-Gebbie-2015:Inferring}, in which deglacial mixed layer
$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ was inferred from benthic
records via the same circulation model and compared to planktonic
estimates. In that work, a discrete history at each surface grid cell
is sought, and the singular value decomposition of the model matrix is
used to reduce the degrees of freedom in the problem. That approach
results in spatiotemporal modes, while our approach computes spatial
modes that are then propagated through an ocean circulation model and
used to form the model matrix. In addition,
\citeA{Amrhein-Gebbie-2015:Inferring} used globally distributed
sediment cores, while this work uses a newly-generated compilation of
high-resolution North Atlantic sediment cores. Finally, our approach
decomposes the measured $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ into
temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$, whereas
\citeA{Amrhein-Gebbie-2015:Inferring} inferred surface mixed layer
equilibrium $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$.

Prior inverse techniques have only incorporated the central estimate
from age models and not the full
age model ensemble \cite[]{Burke-Marchal-2011:Application,
  Gebbie-2012:Tracer, Amrhein-Gebbie-2015:Inferring}. Outside of
inverse methods, age model uncertainty has previously been
incorporated into frequency and correlation analysis
\cite[]{Khider-Emile-Geay-2022:Pyleoclim}, but has not, to the
knowledge of the authors, been taken into account in an inverse
framework. Here, we develop an approach to convert a representation of
the first and second moments of age model uncertainty into
$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ space, which can be propagated
through the problem.

\section{Methods}
Here we quantify the relationship between time-varying surface
behavior, modified and propagated by circulation to depth where it is
``observed'' at sediment core sites by benthic foraminifera. We use the Bayesian age model ensembles and
$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ measurements from
\citeA{Lu-Oppo-2023:Surface} to generate our observational constraints
and associated uncertainties (Section \ref{sec:y}). Deep interior ocean signals are directly
related to preceding surface boundary conditions via an ocean
circulation model (Section \ref{sec:prop}). We form a reduced set of
surface boundary conditions informed by the water mass composition of
the sediment core sites (Section \ref{sec:spatmode}). Finally, we
invert for the time-varying magnitudes of the surface boundary
condition via a statistical solution technique (Section
\ref{sec:solution}). A table of all variable definitions, units, and
dimensions is provided in Supplemental Table \ref{supp-var}.

\subsection{Benthic foraminiferal observations \label{sec:y}}
In this inversion, we are solving for the evolution of surface
properties that best explain the collective behavior of 11
high-resolution benthic foraminiferal
$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ sediment core records previously
described in \citeA[]{Lu-Oppo-2023:Surface}. These records were
collected from along the Reykjanes Ridge offshore of Iceland, and form
a depth transect from 1100-2300m. The
$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ measurements were obtained from
\textit{Uvigerina peregrina} (\textit{U. peregrina}) tests for the
three shallowest cores (MC28A, MC26A, and MC25A), and from
\textit{Cibicidoides wuellerstorfi} (\textit{C. wuellerstorfi}) tests
for the other eight sediment cores.

The measured $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ of a foraminiferal
test is dependent upon the ambient temperature and
$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ at the time of calcification
\cite[]{Urey-1947, McCrea-1950:Isotopic}. The
$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ is in turn primarily affected
by evaporation and precipitation such that higher
$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ is associated with saltier
waters, while lower $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ is
associated with fresher waters \cite[]{Emiliani--1955:Pleistocene,
  Bemis-Spero-1998:Reevaluation}. Ultimately, higher values of
$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ are associated with either
cooler temperatures and/or more saline waters. In particular, the
$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ of foraminiferal tests can be
linearly approximated \cite{McCrea-1950:Isotopic},
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:cibs}
\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}} = \alpha T + \beta + \delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}},
\end{equation}
where $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ and
$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ are assumed to be relative to
$\permil$ Pee Dee Belemnite
(PDB). Specifically, $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ is converted from Vienna
Standard Mean Ocean Water (VSMOW) to PDB with a 0.27$\permil$ offset
\cite[]{<new-entry2>}. Here we use the constants
$\alpha = -0.224 \pm 0.002$ $\permil\mathrm{ K}^{-1}$ and
$\beta = 3.53 \pm 0.02\permil$ for \textit{C. wuellerstorfi} tests
\cite{marchitto}. While \textit{C. wuellerstorfi} calcifies at
equilibrium, \textit{U. peregrina} is isotopically heavier by
0.47$\permil$ \cite[]{marchitto} and we remove this equilibration
offset before applying Equation (\ref{eq:cibs}).

\citeA{Lu-Oppo-2023:Surface} used the Bacon software package
\cite{Bacon, McKay-Emile-Geay-2021:R} to generate a Bayesian
accumulation history for each sediment core record based on 3-8
radiocarbon samples and an assigned coretop age, resulting in an age model for each sediment core that consists of 1000 realizations of age with
respect to depth. The $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ series is aligned to each age model realization, providing an
ensemble of $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ measurements with
varying age evolution. Here, we sample the
$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ ensemble to produce a
distribution of possible $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ values
at any given time. From the age model ensemble, we calculate the first
and second moments. The central estimate (i.e., first moment) of the
$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ distribution at any time
$t$ is used to make the observational constraint, referred
to as $\mathbf{y}$. In addition, we will use these distributions to
compute the $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ covariance (i.e.,
second moment) between any two times, which we use
to make the age model covariance matrix, $\mathbf{C}_{\mathrm{age}}$
(\ref{sec:agemodel}).

This approach extends prior methodologies
\cite[]{LeGrand-Wunsch-1995:Constraints, Gebbie2006,
  Marchal-Curry-2008:Abyssal, Amrhein-Gebbie-2015:Inferring}, which
used the central estimate of the age model to temporally place the
$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ measurements, but did not
otherwise account for age model uncertainty. Instead, they use a
higher estimate of the observational uncertainty (0.2$\permil$) than
most laboratory measurements (0.07$\permil$), under the assumption
that the overestimate will capture uncertainties in the interpretation
of the proxy \cite{Gebbie2006}. Here, we attempt to accurately account
for the quantifiable sources of uncertainty, namely, a laboratory
measurement uncertainty of 0.07$\permil$ \cite[]{Lu-Oppo-2023:Surface}
and an age model covariance matrix, $\mathbf{C}_{\mathrm{age}}$. These
two sources of uncertainty are combined into a representation of the
first and second moments of observational uncertainty,
$\mathbf{C_{yy}}$ (complete details in \ref{sec:agemodel}) and
propagated through the problem.

Once the observational vector, $\mathbf{y}$, is formed, we compare it
with an estimate of effective modern-day
$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ (i.e., what a hypothetical
foraminifera would record if it were exposed to some given temperature
and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$). We find that the depth
structure in recorded CE $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ is
largely captured by modern-day estimates of
$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$, although offsets exist
especially at shallower depths (Figure \ref{fig:offset}). These
offsets are large compared to the CE variability recorded in the
sediment core records.  Further details on effective modern-day
$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ and interpretation of Figure
\ref{fig:offset} are in Supplemental Section \ref{supp-sec:offset}.

Due to uncertainty in the equilibration offset of
\textit{U. peregrina} \cite[]{Shackleton1974AttainmentOI, marchitto},
as well as uncertainty in modern day estimates of
$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ at depth, we choose to work in
$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ anomaly space, as was done in
\citeA{Amrhein-Gebbie-2015:Inferring}.
This ensures that we maximize the fit to the coherent temporal CE
signal, without contamination by the mean offset at each core site,
which we believe to be partially affected by species offsets. We
convert all quantities into anomalies by subtracting the mean value
between 1850-1980 CE from each record.

\subsection{Tracer transport model \label{sec:prop}}
Ambient waters at an interior ocean
location can be related to surface waters at some previous time via an
ocean circulation model
\cite{Burke-Marchal-2011:Application,Gebbie-2012:Tracer,Amrhein-Gebbie-2015:Inferring}. Here,
we use the empirical ocean circulation model of
\citeA[]{Gebbie-Huybers-2012:mean}, in which the steady-state
water-mass model of \citeA{gebbie2010} was extended by incorporating
radiocarbon observations. This provides a discrete time tracer
transport operator, $\mathbf{L}$, that quantifies the relationship
between all interior locations and surface locations as a function of
mass fluxes. The matrix, $\mathbf{L}$, can be used to compute the time
evolution of a three-dimensional tracer field, $\mathbf{c}$, as
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:diffeq}
\mathbf{c}(t + \Delta t) = \mathbf{Lc}(t) + \mathbf{Bq}(t), 
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{B}$ is a Dirichlet boundary condition mapping matrix,
$\mathbf{q}(t)$ is a prescribed surface boundary condition that can be
time-varying, and $\Delta t$ is an arbitrary timestep from time $t$. We choose a timestep of $\Delta t = 10$ years, which is slightly less than the average radiocarbon age uncertainty of $\sim$50 years. 

As previously noted, $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ is
controlled by the temperature and
$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ of the ambient water at the
time of calcification. We combine the tracer transport model
Equation (\ref{eq:diffeq}) with the paleotemperature Equation
(\ref{eq:cibs}) to form a boundary propagator equation for the
effective $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ at any interior
location due to time-evolving
surface boundary conditions,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:diffeqspec}
\mathbf{c_{c}}(t+\Delta t) = \alpha[\mathbf{L} \mathbf{c_{T}}(t) + \mathbf{B} \mathbf{q_{T}}(t)] + \mathbf{L}\mathbf{c_{s}}(t) + \mathbf{B}\mathbf{q_{s}}(t). 
\end{equation}
Again all quantities are anomalies from 1850-1980 CE, so the constants
$\beta$ and 0.27$\permil$ from Equation (\ref{eq:cibs}) are not included
here. The vector, $\mathbf{c_c}$, is vectorized
$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ at all interior locations,
$\mathbf{c_{T}}$ is vectorized temperature at all interior locations,
and $\mathbf{c_{s}}$ is vectorized
$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ at all interior locations. The
vector, $\mathbf{q_{T}}$, is the temperature boundary condition at all
surface locations, and $\mathbf{q_{s}}$ is the
$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ boundary condition at all
surface locations.

Equation (\ref{eq:diffeqspec}) relies on the assumption that
$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ is near-conservative
\cite[]{Lynch-Stieglitz-2003:Tracers} and can be treated as a passive
tracer. In addition, we assume that perturbations to temperature are
small enough to not detectably affect the circulation, allowing us to
also treat temperature as a passive tracer as in
\citeA{gebbie19}. However, \citeA[]{Scheen-Stocker-2020:Effect} used
an intermediate complexity Earth system model to demonstrate that SST
variations of the magnitude reported by \citeA{gebbie19} can cause
changes in ocean circulation that would explain about one-third of
deep ocean heat content changes of LIA cooling, which are not captured
here.

An interpolation matrix, $\mathbf{O}$, is used to ``observe'' the
effective $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ field,
$\mathbf{c_{c}}$, at the locations of the sediment cores, resulting in
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:yeff}
\mathbf{y}(t) = \mathbf{O}\mathbf{c_{c}}(t). 
\end{equation}
Here, $\mathbf{y}(t)$ is the vectorized effective
$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ at the sediment core sites
according to some propagated surface boundary conditions,
$\mathbf{q_{T}}$ and $\mathbf{q_{s}}$. We ultimately seek optimized values of
$\mathbf{q_{s}}(t)$ and $\mathbf{q_{T}}(t)$ for all times relevant to
the sediment core records.

Equations (\ref{eq:diffeqspec}) and (\ref{eq:yeff}) relate signals in
the interior $(\mathbf{y}(t+\Delta t))$ to the surface
$(\mathbf{q_{T}}(t), \mathbf{q_{s}}(t))$, but also have dependence on
prior interior tracer concentrations $(\mathbf{c_{c}}(t))$. This
requires a time-stepping approach to solve that is computationally
intensive. To circumvent a time-stepping approach, we apply a boundary
propagator technique to remove the direct dependence on prior interior
tracer concentrations in Equation (\ref{eq:diffeqspec}) (see
Supplemental Section \ref{supp-sec:ttm} and
\citeA[]{Haine-Griffies-2024:Review} for more discussion).

We seek a formulation that
infers effective $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ values at the
sediment core sites at all relevant times, based upon all surface
boundary conditions, $\mathbf{q_{T}}(t)$ and $\mathbf{q_{s}}(t)$, at
all relevant times. We refer to these as the vectors
$\mathbf{\hat{y}}$, $\mathbf{\hat{q}_{T}}$, and
$\mathbf{\hat{q}_{s}}$, which are the time-concatenated version of the
vectors in Equation \ref{eq:diffeqspec}, as in
\begin{align}
  \label{eq:expand} 
\mathbf{\hat{q}_{T}} = \begin{bmatrix}
                         \mathbf{q_{T}}(t = 1980)\\
                         \mathbf{q_{T}}(t = 1970)\\
                         \vdots \\
                         \mathbf{q_{T}}(t = 500)
                        \end{bmatrix},
\mathbf{\hat{q}_{s}} = \begin{bmatrix}
                         \mathbf{q_{s}}(t = 1980)\\
                         \mathbf{q_{s}}(t = 1970)\\
                         \vdots \\
                         \mathbf{q_{s}}(t = 500)
                        \end{bmatrix},
\mathbf{\hat{y}} = \begin{bmatrix}
                         \mathbf{y}(t = 1980)\\
                         \mathbf{y}(t = 1970)\\
                         \vdots \\
                         \mathbf{y}(t = 800)
                         \end{bmatrix}.                         
\end{align}
Note that the time-concatenated surface boundary vector,
$\mathbf{\hat{q}}$, is defined on a time period that precedes the
beginning of the time period that $\mathbf{\hat{y}}$ is
defined in order to account for the lagged response of interior
signals to surface signals. Specifically, $\mathbf{\hat{q}}$ is
defined from 500-1980 CE, and $\mathbf{\hat{y}}$ is
defined from 800-1980 CE, both with a timestep of $\Delta t = 10$ years.

We combine Equations (\ref{eq:diffeqspec}) and (\ref{eq:yeff}) as
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:modeleq}
\mathbf{\hat{y}} = \mathbf{G}' (\alpha \mathbf{\hat{q}_{T}} + \mathbf{\hat{q}_{s}}), 
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{G}'$ is the boundary propagator matrix that relates all
surface grid cells to interior sediment core locations, for all times
of interest. The matrix, $\mathbf{G'}$, is sparse due to the causal
relation between the input and output.
Each row of $\mathbf{G'}$ corresponds to the set of boundary propagator
functions, for all surface grid cells in $\mathbf{\hat{q}}$, at a
specific sediment core and specific time. A boundary propagator
function is defined here as the response at any interior location in
the ocean to a brief surface signal at some preceding time.
For a North Atlantic surface signal,
most of the signal arrives at the sediment core sites within 100 years
of the initial surface signal due to the fast timescale of deepwater
formation in this region \cite{Lu-Oppo-2023:Surface}.

The matrix, $\mathbf{G}'$, will have the same number of columns and
sediment cores (11) multiplied by the number of timesteps (52), or
572. The number of rows will be the number of grid cells in each
surface boundary condition, $\mathbf{\hat{q}}$ (11,113), multiplied by
the number of time steps in $\mathbf{\hat{q}}$ (149), or
$1.7 \times 10^{6}$. These large matrices pose significant computational
challenges, so we seek to reduce the dimensionality of this problem next.

\subsection{Surface spatial modes \label{sec:spatmode}}
Here the problem is reduced by defining characteristic surface
boundary conditions, $\mathbf{v}_{i}$, that have a time-varying
magnitude, $u_{i}(t)$. This set of reduced surface boundary conditions
is termed the spatial modes, $\mathbf{v}_{i}$, and their
associated magnitudes are the mode magnitudes, $u_{i}$. Any
arbitrary surface boundary condition vector, $\mathbf{q}(t)$, is
decomposed into a superposition of $N$ spatial modes scaled by their
associated mode magnitudes, as in,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:surfbc}
\mathbf{q}(t) = \sum_{i}^{N} u_{i}(t) \mathbf{v}_{i}. 
\end{equation}
Similarly, the vector, $\mathbf{\hat{u}}$, concatenates all mode
magnitudes at all times. This simplification allows us to reformulate
Equation (\ref{eq:modeleq}) as
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:modeleqfin}
\mathbf{\mathbf{\hat{y}}} = \mathbf{M}'(\alpha \mathbf{\hat{u}_{T}} + \mathbf{\hat{u}_{s}}). 
\end{equation}
The matrix, $\mathbf{M}'$, is a boundary propagator for
constant-in-time spatial modes, $\mathbf{v}_{i}$, from which we infer
the time-varying mode magnitudes $\mathbf{\hat{u}_{T}}$ and
$\mathbf{\hat{u}_{s}}$. The number of columns in our boundary
propagator matrix is reduced from $1.7\times10^{6}$ to $149N$, where $N$
is the chosen number of spatial modes. Equation (\ref{eq:modeleqfin})
combines both the proxy system model and the lagged response at the
core sites due to ocean circulation into one equation (Supplemental Section \ref{supp-sec:ttm}).

We make several simplifying assumptions in order to arrive at a
minimal set of surface spatial modes that provide a diverse possible
solution space. First, we take advantage of the large spatial scales
relevant to atmospheric and surface ocean dynamics
\cite <e.g.,>{Stammer-1997:Global}, and seek a spatially smooth solution
that resolves surface features that are approximately
$>100$ km. In addition, we prioritize surface regions that contribute
more water to the sediment core sites, such as deepwater formation regions.
To further simplify this problem, we assume that temperature and
$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ have similar spatial
variability, allowing us to use the same set of surface spatial modes to
characterize both temperature and
$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ variability. This assumption is based upon the
correlation between
$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ and salinity, implying that
common oceanographically-relevant water masses will have coherent
temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$. To generate
surface spatial modes that fulfill these simplifications, we identify
orthonormal surface spatial modes based on the singular value
decomposition of surface water origination maps for each sediment
core, obtained via a steady-state water-mass model (Supplemental
Section \ref{supp-sec:spatmode}). We arrive at a set
of 11 surface spatial modes, limited by the number of sediment core
records in the dataset. Our $\mathbf{M}'$ matrix will
have 1639 columns, down from $1.7 \times 10^{6}$ columns that would be required to invert for each
surface grid cell, which increases computational tractability.

Modes are shown in Figure \ref{fig:svdmodes}, where the first mode
explains $\approx$99\% of the variability in source water origination maps
for all core sites. Mode 1 characterizes the general surface region
that is most important to the sediment core sites, and consists of a
single-signed signal with maximum magnitude located directly north of
Iceland. This signal is strong across most of the Norwegian and
Iceland Seas. Mode 2, which explains $\approx$1\% of the variability in source water
origination maps for all core sites, is characterized by a signal
centered in the Nordic Seas, and an anti-phased signal in the Labrador
and Irminger Seas. This implies that source water origination maps for
each core site tend to have an anti-phased contribution between these two regions (e.g., a sediment core
location that has more water originating from the Nordic Seas will
have less water originating from the Labrador and Irminger Seas, and
vice versa).

Although Mode 1 explains almost all of the variability in the surface
origination maps, this does not necessarily imply that it is the only
surface mode needed to explain the sediment core data. The left singular vectors, or unscaled principal components, of the surface source water origination maps,
have a consistently-valued negative contribution
towards every sediment core site for Mode 1 (Supplemental Figure
\ref{supp-fig:U}). Thus, we cannot explain
any depth-dependent variability using solely Mode 1.
This approach minimizes the degrees of freedom in the problem, while
maximizing the amount of information our optimized estimate contains
by quantifying the spatially-varying contribution of the surface ocean
to the sediment core sites.

Once the spatial modes are identified, we compute the associated
boundary propagator (Supplemental Section \ref{supp-sec:ttm}). This system can be
integratively solved at any timestep via a quasi-constant timestep
numerical differentiation function (QNDF) solver
\cite[]{doi:10.1137/S1064827594276424}. We compute each boundary propagator function over a period that exceeds the greatest
expected transport timescale (2,000 years), at a resolution that smoothly captures
the signal propagation (10 years).

Mode 1's boundary propagator function has nearly
identical evolution at the sediment core sites (Supplemental Figure
\ref{supp-fig:modemags}), in line with the finding that it has similar
influence over all sediment core sites. Mode 2's boundary propagator function demonstrates that the positive-signed anomaly north of
Iceland in Mode 2 is more important for deep sediment cores, while the
negative-signed anomaly south of Iceland affects the intermediate
sediment core sites. This further reinforces our choice to retain all
11 modes in the reduced SVD, as the modes which explain a low
percentage of the variance in surface source water origination maps
could still explain depth-varying behavior in the sediment core records.
\subsection{Solution method \label{sec:solution}}
We seek to construct a classical inverse problem of the format,
$\mathbf{\hat{y}} = \mathbf{E\hat{u}}$, in which the observation
vector of $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ anomalies,
$\mathbf{\hat{y}}$, is related to the temperature and
$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ surface spatial mode
magnitudes, $\mathbf{\hat{u}}$, via a model matrix, $\mathbf{E}$. The
vector $\mathbf{\hat{u}}$ is formed from the concatenation of
$\mathbf{\hat{u_{T}}}$ and $\mathbf{\hat{u}_{s}}$. The vector
$\mathbf{\hat{y}}$ contains all sediment core observations, and is the
known quantity in this problem, while the state vector
$\mathbf{\hat{u}}$ is the unknown quantity that will be computed via
inversion. The model matrix, $\mathbf{E}$, quantifies the effect of
perturbations in $\mathbf{\hat{u_{T}}}$ or $\mathbf{\hat{u_{s}}}$ on
each sediment core record observation in $\mathbf{\hat{y}}$ via the
matrix $\mathbf{M'}$ in Equation (\ref{eq:modeleqfin}). Individual
rows of the model matrix $\mathbf{E}$ can be identified by making
small perturbations to an arbitrary state vector, $\mathbf{\hat{u}}$,
applying Equation (\ref{eq:modeleqfin}), and scaling the result by the
perturbation.

In this underdetermined problem, we further constrain the solution
through a prior estimate of $\mathbf{\hat{u}}$, referred to as
$\mathbf{\hat{u_{0}}}$. Both the observational vector,
$\mathbf{\hat{y}}$, and the prior, $\mathbf{\hat{u_{0}}}$, are
anomalies from the 1850-1980 CE mean. When the prior is propagated
through the model matrix, $\mathbf{E\hat{u_{0}}}$, the mean value of
$\mathbf{\hat{y}}$ during 1850-1980 should be zero (i.e., equal to the
mean). Therefore, we set the prior to be null ($\mathbf{\hat{u_0}}$ =
$\vec{0}$).

We control how tightly the estimated mode magnitudes,
$\mathbf{\hat{\tilde{u}}}$, are constrained to the prior,
$\mathbf{\hat{u_{0}}}$, via the prior's covariance matrix,
$\mathbf{C_{u_{0}u_{0}}}$ (Supplemental Section
\ref{supp-sec:Cuu}). This matrix controls the prior in
three ways. First, we constrain the variance of the mode magnitudes
associated with temperature by the estimated temporal variance of each
surface spatial mode magnitude for temperature from coupled climate
model output. Unlike sea
surface temperature, there are no time-varying observational gridded
estimates of $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$, and modeled
$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ products are still quite novel
\cite[]{Brady-Stevenson-2019:Connected}. For this reason, we control
the variance of the mode magnitudes associated with
$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ by scaling the estimated
temperature variance by the estimated
temperature:$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ correlation of
$\gamma = 14.8$ K/$\permil$, which we estimate using isotope-enabled couple
climate model output in \ref{sec:corr}. We also control the
temperature:$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ correlation in our
estimate with this value, as a separate constraint in the
$\mathbf{C_{u_{0}u_{0}}}$ matrix. In our analysis of of the
correlation constant, $\gamma$, we note that there are multiple approaches
to estimate this value, and present a sensitivity analysis of our
results in Section \ref{sec:gammasens}.

We solve the inverse problem for the time-varying magnitudes of
surface spatial modes, $\mathbf{\hat{u}}$, by using the Gauss-Markov
Theorem. This methodology minimizes the uncertainty of the estimate
\cite[]{Wunsch-2006:Discrete}. Equivalently, the following cost
function is minimized,
\begin{equation}  
\label{eq:costfunc}
\tilde{J} = \left(\mathbf{\hat{y}}-\mathbf{\tilde{\hat{y}}}\right)^T\mathbf{C_{yy}}^{-1}\left(\mathbf{\hat{y}}-\mathbf{\tilde{\hat{y}}}\right)+(\mathbf{\tilde{\hat{u}} - \hat{u_0}})^T\mathbf{C_{u_{0}u_{0}}}^{-1}(\mathbf{\tilde{\hat{u}} - \hat{u_0}}), 
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{\tilde{\hat{y}}}$ is the estimated values at the core
sites according to $\mathbf{\tilde{\hat{u}}}$
(e.g. $\mathbf{\tilde{\hat{y}}} = \mathbf{E\tilde{\hat{u}}}$).

We solve for mode magnitudes, $\mathbf{\tilde{\hat{u}}}$, that minimize $d\tilde{J}$/$d\mathbf{\tilde{\hat{u}}}$, 
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{\tilde{\hat{u}}} = \mathbf{\hat{u_{0}}} + \mathbf{C_{u_{0}u_{0}}} \mathbf{E}^T\left(\mathbf{E}\mathbf{C_{u_{0}u_{0}}}\mathbf{E}^T + \mathbf{C_{yy}}\right)^{-1}\left(\mathbf{\hat{y}}-\mathbf{E\hat{u_0}}\right)
\label{eq:twls}.
\end{equation}
The associated covariance can be computed according to
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{C_{\tilde{u}\tilde{u}}} = \mathbf{C_{u_0u_0}}-\mathbf{C_{u_0u_0}}\mathbf{E}^T(\mathbf{E}\mathbf{C_{u_0u_0}}\mathbf{E}^T + \mathbf{C_{yy}}) \mathbf{E}\mathbf{C_{u_0u_0}}.
\label{eq:twlsCuu}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Summary of Methods \label{sec:som}}
In summary, we have proposed a methodology that produces the temporal
evolution of distinct surface modes of variability that best explain
benthic sediment core data. This methodology propagates a full
representation of the first and second statistical moments of the
observations throughout the problem. For the first time, we
simultaneously account for factors such as the age model uncertainty,
the competing effects of temperature and
$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ upon
$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$, and the modification and lag of
surface signals due to ocean circulation.

\section{Results}
Here, we present two estimates of temperature and
$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$, differentiated by their
temporal extent and observational constraints. For each estimate, the
included sediment core records are trimmed to the common time period
shared by all sediment cores in order to maintain numerical
stability. We refer to the estimate that includes all 11 records in
the \citeA[]{Lu-Oppo-2023:Surface} dataset as the \texttt{OPT-11}
estimate, which is constrained by data from 1470-1980 CE. This
estimate does not cover the beginning of the LIA, and is therefore not
suitable for estimating quantities such as the magnitude of LIA
cooling. In order to capture the onset of the LIA, we present the
\texttt{OPT-3} estimate, which fits sediment cores MC26A, MC22A, and
MC13A. These three sediment cores, which have the largest temporal
extent (800-1980 CE) within this dataset, sample intermediate (MC26A:
1031 m), deep (MC22A: 1535 m), and abyssal (MC13A: 2129 m)
waters. This estimate is suitable for assessing the magnitude of
MCA-LIA cooling, but is not expected to fit all 11 sediment cores with
as high fidelity as \texttt{OPT-11}.

In Figure \ref{fig:utilde}, the long-term monotonic trend in Mode 1 in
the \texttt{OPT-3} estimate starting around 800 CE is explained by a long-term cooling
trend. Note that Mode 1 is a negative North Atlantic anomaly, so a
positive trend in the mode magnitude implies cooling. In the
\texttt{OPT-11} estimate, more of the industrial warming signal is
captured than in the \texttt{OPT-3} estimate. The \texttt{OPT-11}
estimate captures less of the temporal extent of the MCA-LIA related
cooling, as the record begins at 1470 CE. A distinct maxima at
$\sim$1800 CE for Mode 1 is present in \texttt{OPT-11}, which is followed
by an industrial warming signal.

The relationship between the optimized mode magnitudes and
observations is defined via the boundary propagator functions of each
surface spatial mode interpolated at each sediment core sites
(Supplemental Figure \ref{supp-fig:modemags}). Most boundary propagator
functions have initial signal peaks between 10-25 years from the
initial signal, and return to null values by 100 years. This means
that an observation has the most
influence over the estimated surface conditions 10-25 years
earlier and little influence for 100 or more years earlier. This explains some relevant numerical features of the
optimized mode magnitudes in Figure \ref{fig:utilde}, such as the
unconstrained values (i.e., $\mathbf{\tilde{\hat{u}}} = 0$) before the
existence of data in $\mathbf{\hat{y}}$. The mode magnitude vector,
$\mathbf{\hat{u}}$, is defined over a longer time period than the
observational vector, $\mathbf{\hat{y}}$, to account for the circulation-induced lag between the sea surface and core sites. There is no information to
estimate the surface conditions a few hundred years before the earliest observation becomes available, so the estimated mode magnitude, $\mathbf{\tilde{\hat{u}}}$, reverts to the prior,
$\mathbf{\hat{u_0}}$. Similarly, the optimized value at the final timestep also reverts
to the prior because
there has not been enough time for that signal to propagate to depth and be recorded in the sediment core.

\subsection{Reconstruction at sediment core sites \label{sec:reconcore}}
By propagating all optimized surface spatial mode magnitudes,
$\mathbf{\tilde{\hat{u}}}$, through the model matrix, $\mathbf{E}$, we
can generate timeseries of the reconstructed observations at each
sediment core site, $\mathbf{\tilde{\hat{y}}}$, as shown in Figure
\ref{fig:recon}. The associated uncertainty is computed according to,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:solcov}
\mathbf{C_{\tilde{y}\tilde{y}}} = \mathbf{E C_{\tilde{u}\tilde{u}}E}^T. 
\end{equation}
Although sediment cores MC28A, MC25A, MC21A, MC20A,
MC19A, MC10A, MC9A, and MC14A are not observational constraints on
\texttt{OPT-3}, we compute the effective reconstructed values at these
sediment core sites according to \texttt{OPT-3} as cross-validation.

Both estimates fall within the 1$\sigma$ error of the original sediment
core records, confirming that the data is well fit. Centennial-scale
variability, such as that observed in sediment cores MC21A, MC20A, and
MC19A, is not captured in either reconstructed estimate, but this does
not appear to contribute significantly to the cost function value,
most likely due to the signal-to-noise ratio. The \texttt{OPT-11}
estimate fits all sediment cores slightly better than \texttt{OPT-3},
especially for the sediment cores that are not observational
constraints on \texttt{OPT-3}. We interpret \texttt{OPT-11} as a more
data-informed result for the past 500 years, whereas \texttt{OPT-3} is
informed by less data, but extends over 1000 years. In Figure
\ref{fig:utilde}, a more sustained period of peak Little Ice Age
cooling was noted in \texttt{OPT-11} than in \texttt{OPT-3}. The
sediment core reconstruction shows that sediment cores MC21A, MC20A,
MC10A, and MC9A (which are all constraints on \texttt{OPT-11} but not
\texttt{OPT-3}) exhibit this more sustained Little Ice Age, hence the
difference between the two estimates.

Although many of the sediment records from cores in similar depth
ranges have disparate mean signals (i.e., MC19A and MC10A), our
estimates are a fairly ``middle-of-the-road'' compromise between all
signals, in which the reconstruction is fairly similar between
neighboring core sites and seeks to optimize a common signal from all
sediment core records. This result is in accordance with Mode 1 being
the only constrained mode, as it is a single-signed surface anomaly
with a similar time evolution across all sediment cores sites.

\subsection{Surface reconstructions \label{sec:surfrecon}}
Output surface maps of temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ throughout time can be constructed by propagating estimated spatial mode magnitudes, at a specific time, $t_u$, and for a specific state variable, $s$, through the $\mathbf{V}$ matrix, as in
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:surfrecon}
  \mathbf{\tilde{s}}[t_u, s] = \mathbf{V \tilde{\hat{u}}}[t_u, s]. 
\end{equation}
The associated covariance matrix is,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:surfreconunc}
\mathbf{C_{\tilde{s}\tilde{s}}}[t_u, s] = \mathbf{V C_{\tilde{u}\tilde{u}}}[t_u, s]\mathbf{V}^T.
\end{equation}

In Figure \ref{fig:meants}, we present the mean high-latitude North Atlantic
(50$^{\circ}$W-20$^{\circ}$E, 50$^{\circ}$N-90$^{\circ}$N) temperature series for
\texttt{OPT-3} and \texttt{OPT-11} and compare to the mean
temperature series in all 20 realizations of the
Last Millennium Reanalysis (LMR) \cite[]{Hakim-Emile-Geay-2016:last,
  Tardif2019}, as well as the HadISST reanalysis
\cite[]{Rayner-Parker-2003:Global}. Note that outside of this box, the
sea surface has very little influence over the core sites and has
negligible magnitude. We also compare it to the median Ocean2k SST,
computed by scaling the median, 5-, and 95-percentile range
standardized Ocean2k SST by the published global Ocean2k cooling trend
of 0.42 $^{\circ}$C/kyr \cite{ocean2k}.

\section{Discussion}
\subsection{Uniqueness of estimates}
In Section \ref{sec:solchar}, we showed that we were able to fit the
sediment core records by only constraining the Mode 1 surface spatial
mode. Previously, we identified Mode 1 as a mode that could only
explain common signals to all sediment cores. This was supported by
the similar forms of the Mode 1 boundary propagator functions at all
depth levels and the similar values of the Mode 1 left singular values. In Figure \ref{fig:recon}, we find that a common signal is
able to fit all sediment core signals within uncertainty, suggesting
that this sediment core record compilation can be fit without the
necessity of explaining depth-dependent signals.

Are other North Atlantic surface spatial modes besides Mode 1 also able to explain the sediment core
records? And, if not, what are the important characteristics of a

\subsection{Sensitivity of estimate to temperature:$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ correlation \label{sec:gammasens}}
An important parameter in this inversion presented is the
enforced correlation between temperature and
$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$, $\gamma$. Intuitively, we expect
$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ and salinity to be strongly
correlated, as they are both controlled by the same processes
(evaporation, precipitation). We expect some correlation between
temperature and salinity, as cooler waters tend to be fresher and
warmer waters tend to be saltier. Therefore, we also expect some
correlation between temperature and
$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$, but the exact value of the
correlation is unclear.

Variability at an interior ocean location may be caused by (i)
isopycnal transport of anomalies, or (ii) diapycnal transport across
isopycnals. The analysis presented in \ref{sec:corr} is based upon the
correlation between temperature and
$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ at an interior location in an
isotope-enabled coupled climate model, which is intended to
realistically model the effects of both (i) and (ii). However,
arguments may exist for assuming that either (i) or (ii) are the
predominant source of interior variability during the CE. As an
example, if we assumed that diapycnal transport (i.e., isopycnals
heaving) were the primary source of interior variability during the
CE, we may use a different approach to estimate the
temperature:$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ correlation
value. In this extreme, we would expect interior variability to be
more related to the depth structure of temperature, salinity, and
$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$. In Supplemental Section
\ref{supp-sec:oxygenvar}, we estimate a correlation value of
$\gamma = 36.8$ K$\permil^{-1}$, which is much higher than the value used
in the main text of this paper ($\gamma = 14.8$ K$\permil^{-1}$).

We have shown that $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ variations at
depth can be well-explained by the variability of a single North
Atlantic surface mode. The simplicity of this conclusion allows us to
form a one-equation linear relation for the amount of surface water
temperature change required to explain a
$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ change at depth. We can
substitute the correlation relationship,
$T = \gamma \delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$, into Equation (\ref{eq:cibs}), arriving at,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:2}
\Delta T = \frac{\Delta \delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}}{\alpha + \gamma^{-1}},
\end{equation}
which solves for the magnitude of temperature change ($\Delta T$) required
to explain some mean interior $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ change
($\Delta \delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$), as a function of the
temperature coefficient from the paleotemperature equation,
$\alpha = -0.224 \permil$/K, and the correlation coefficient,
$\gamma$. If we assume a MCA-LIA
$\Delta \delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}} = 0.08 \permil$, this model
estimates a temperature change of $\Delta T = -0.5$ K, which is in-line with
our estimated mean high-latitude North Atlantic temperature change of 0.41 $\pm$ 0.13
K. If we increase the correlation coefficient to $\gamma = 36.8$ K
$\permil^{-1}$, Equation (\ref{eq:2}) estimates a cooling of $0.40$ K.
As $\gamma$ increases, the required cooling decreases
towards a limit value of $0.36$ K. In Supplemental Figure
\ref{supp-fig:gamma}, we plot Equation (\ref{eq:2}) for reasonable
values of $\gamma$, and show that there is very little sensitivity for
values of $\gamma >$10 K$\permil^{-1}$. However, there is a critical point,
where $\gamma_{\mathrm{crit}} = \alpha^{-1} = 4.45$ K$\permil^{-1}$, and as
$\gamma$ decreases towards this point, MCA-LIA cooling will increase
substantially.

In order for the correlation constant, $\gamma$, to approach the critical
value, either the temperature:salinity correlation constant, $\gamma_{s}$, or salinity:$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ correlation constant, $\gamma_{t}$, must decrease significantly, which
would require substantial changes in either sea ice processes or
hydroclimate processes. Ice-rafted debris records from the Common Era
indicate a subtle increase in the import of more Arctic-origin ice
over the past millennium
\cite[]{Alonso-Garcia-Kleiven-2017:Freshening}. A review of Arctic
hydroclimate records did not identify any significant trend in
hydroclimate index over the past 900 years, but significant regional
behavior was observed \cite[]{Linderholm-Nicolle-2018:Arctic}. It
remains unclear if changes in the sea ice or hydroclimate over the CE
caused temporal variations in
$\gamma$ that would affect our estimated cooling rates. At the very least, we are confident that no temporal changes in $\gamma$ are required to explain the sediment core records, as the main results of this work are in-line with
independent estimates from \citeA{ocean2k}.

\subsection{Reconstruction at sediment core sites}

The \texttt{OPT-3} and \texttt{OPT-11} estimates both agree, within
uncertainties, with the sediment core records. However, the two
estimates have differing time evolutions, with \texttt{OPT-3} cooling
until the beginning of the 20th century, and \texttt{OPT-11}
exhibiting a sustained LIA that lasted from the mid-1500s until the
beginning of the 20th century. \texttt{OPT-11} represents a slightly
better fit to all sediment core records, and for that reason is our
preferred estimate post-1500. Surface proxy records have not reached
any consensus on the timing or evolution of the MCA-LIA transition,
making it difficult to validate either estimate in this regard
\cite[]{MoffaSanchez:Variability}.

\subsection{Surface reconstructions}

In this work, we have presented a new estimate of North Atlantic
MCA-LIA surface cooling inferred from benthic sediment core
records. Our reconstruction suggests a fairly high magnitude cooling
during the last millennium (0.41 $\pm$ 0.13 K cooling from 1140-1880
CE). \citeA[]{Cunningham-Austin-2013:Reconstruction} reconstructed SST
for the northeast North Atlantic using a composite plus regression
approach to integrate planktonic foraminiferal records, estimating a
MCA-LIA cooling of $0.2^{\circ}$C/kyr that appeared after 1250 CE and
persisted until the early 20th Century. The previous estimate of
global mean SST cooling of 0.42 $^{\circ}$C/kyr \cite{ocean2k} is similar
to our North Atlantic reconstruction. Terrestrial cooling in the
surrounding regions has been estimated to be 0.47 $^{\circ}$C/kyr in the
Arctic, 0.09$^{\circ}$C/kyr in Europe, and 0.34 $^{\circ}$C/kyr in North
America \cite[]{Consortium--2013:Continental}. This mismatch in
terrestrial and marine cooling rates may be a result of seasonal
biases in proxies. In particular, the bottom waters that our benthic
foraminifera record are seasonally biased towards wintertime
conditions \cite[]{Stommel-1979:Determination,
  Williams-Marshall-1995:Does}. Therefore, we interpret this result as
evidence for temperature changes in the regions of deepwater formation
that outpace reconstructed terrestrial MCA-LIA temperature changes,
for all regions besides the Arctic.

In Figure \ref{fig:meants}, we noted considerable disagreement between
the LMR and our reconstructions. The LMR is an offline data
assimilation product, which uses CMIP models to construct a prior and
constrains it through the PAGES2k database
\cite[]{Hakim-Emile-Geay-2016:last}. This disagreement may be caused
by the previously discussed bias in our estimate towards wintertime
bottom-of-mixed layer temperatures, or seasonal biases in the PAGES2k
records. Another potential cause may be the decreasing number of proxy
records towards the beginning of the last millennium, which could
cause dampening of the MCA-LIA cooling signal in LMR (M. Evans,
personal communication, 2024).

In \citeA{gebbie19}, peak North Atlantic LIA was identified in the
1800s, which was noted to be amplified from the global average. This
feature was suggested to be due to a winter-amplified MCA-LIA cooling,
preferentially recorded through deepwater formation.
Our results broadly agree with \citeA{gebbie19} despite using independent data constraints. There are some differences in timing, however. The surface temperature reconstructions of \citeA{gebbie19} exhibit cooling beginning prior to 1000 CE. The planktic stack from \citeA{Lu-Oppo-2023:Surface} may also indicate an earlier onset LIA (Figure \ref{fig:plankticstack}). Our reconstruction and the Ocean2k synthesis, on the other hand, appear to have a later cooling onset. Although this disparity warrants more investigation, it suggests that, in order for the interior ocean to match the Challenger - WOCE warming pattern, and fit the Ocean2k cooling magnitude, an earlier LIA is warranted. 

One explanation for the higher magnitude cooling observed in the North
Atlantic relative to non-Arctic land masses could be the amplification
of Arctic temperature change.  Arctic amplification of the MCA-LIA
cooling has been identified in coupled climate model output
\cite{ottobliesner16}, but has been difficult to identify in
paleoclimate records. Arctic Holocene cooling has been suggested to be
the result of a decrease in summer insolation, which was stronger at
higher latitudes, that was subsequently reinforced by sea-ice and
terrestrial snow feedback mechanisms \cite[]{Kaufman2009}. Similar
feedback mechanisms may have caused the reinforcement of MCA-LIA
cooling in our study region as well, although we cannot directly comment on this possibility.

\subsection{Limitations and further work}

Limitations of this methodology include the enforced correlation
between temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$. In the
future, independent constraints on temperature, such as Mg/Ca records,
could be used to remove or loosen this constraint. In
addition, constraining the expected variance of spatial mode
magnitudes from isotope-enabled climate model output implies that we
are seeking out solutions that have similar ocean statistics to the
simulated historical (1850-2005) ocean. Therefore, our estimates
should be interpreted as possible solutions for surface histories of
$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ and temperature
with similar climatologies to the historical simulation.
% This does not exclude the possibility of a
% reality in which these relationships are different.

The results presented here could be expanded by including more proxy records. This could be accomplished by
incorporating more benthic $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$
records, such as the compilation of \citeA[]{Lund-Curry-2006:Florida},
but could also include adapting the methodology to include constraints
based on surface records. Although North Atlantic CE surface record
exhibit significant disagreement, qualitative regional patterns such
as a East and South Greenland millennial-scale cooling have been
identified \cite[]{MoffaSanchez:Variability}, and could be further
confirmed through an adaption of the presented methodology. In
addition, historical ship-based measurements could be included to
refine the industrial warming estimates, as was presented in
\citeA{gebbie19}.

\section{Conclusion}
Here, we have demonstrated a novel methodology of inferring surface
climate conditions from benthic sediment core records via an ocean
circulation model, while accounting for the lag in transit time from
the surface to depth, age model uncertainty, and the unequal
contributions of temperature and
$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ towards
$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$. In addition, dimension
reduction via a source water decomposition was utilized to increase
the computational tractability of the problem. The effects of
temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ were separated
via established experimental relations and correlations from climate
model output. This methodology resulted in the first 1000-year
reconstruction of SST and
$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ from benthic sediment core
records.

We generated two estimates of high-latitude Common Era North Atlantic temperature
and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$: \texttt{OPT-3}, which
spanned the end of the MCA and the onset of the LIA, and
\texttt{OPT-11}, which captured only the peak of the LIA and the
subsequent recovery toward industrial conditions. \texttt{OPT-3} was
only constrained by three sediment core records, but extended from
800-1970 CE, while \texttt{OPT-11} was constrained by 11 sediment core
records, but only extended from 1470-1980 CE. Both estimates fit the
sediment core records within error, with the main difference being
that \texttt{OPT-11} reconstructed a sustained LIA, while
\texttt{OPT-3} reconstructed a MCA-LIA cooling that persisted until
the early 20th century. We interpret the sustained LIA of the
\texttt{OPT-11} estimate as being the more likely estimate for the
post-1470 CE, but the \texttt{OPT-3} estimate is still suitable for
estimating the magnitude of high-latitude ($>$50$^{\circ}$N) North Atlantic
MCA-LIA cooling (0.41 $\pm$ 0.13 K cooling from 1140-1880 CE).

We found that, in both estimates, the data could be well fit by using
the primary mode of surface water origination at the sediment core
sites, referred to here as Mode 1, which was characterized by a strong
anomaly north of Iceland. Although other modes of variability, such as
a uniform surface pattern, are able to fit the data, the Mode 1
spatial pattern was the only investigated mode that both fit the
sediment core data, and generated temperature reconstructions that
were in agreement with independent reconstructions. This spatial
pattern of cooling was also observed in planktonic SST records in the
region of interest.

Our results support a high-latitude North Atlantic MCA-LIA cooling with a magnitude
that agrees with recent marine proxy compilations \cite{ocean2k} and
Arctic terrestrial cooling estimates
\cite{Consortium--2013:Continental}. This reconstructed cooling
outweighs some marine planktonic reconstructions
\cite[]{Cunningham-Austin-2013:Reconstruction}, as well as estimates
of North American and European terrestrial cooling
\cite{Consortium--2013:Continental}. Such a large Little Ice Age would increase the odds that remnant  preindustrial water masses are detectable in the abyss today.

\section{Incorporating age model uncertainty \label{sec:agemodel}}
For every sediment core, we generate mean timeseries of
$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ at evenly-spaced time intervals
to populate $\mathbf{y}$ by applying the Bacon age models
\cite[]{Bacon, McKay-Emile-Geay-2021:R} from
\citeA{Lu-Oppo-2023:Surface}. Each age model realization was linearly
interpolated at a common 10-year resolution. Then, for each 10-year
timestep, the mean $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ was computed
for any interpolated timestep that had more than 100 realizations
available, populating $\mathbf{y}$. For a specific sediment core, the
associated age model covariance matrix, $\mathbf{C_{\mathrm{age}}}$,
is constructed by computing the covariance between any two timesteps
$t_{1}, t_{2}$,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:covariance}
  \mathbf{C_{\mathrm{age}}}[t_1, t_2] = \frac{\sum_i^M \left(\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_i(t_1) - <\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}(t_1)>
    \right) \left( \delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_i(t_2) - <\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}(t_2)> \right) }{M}, 
\end{equation}
where $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{i}(t_1)$ refers to the interpolated
$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ value at $t_{1}$ for realization
$i$, $<\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}(t_1)>$ is the mean value across all
realizations at timestep $t_1$, and $M$ is the total number of
realizations with interpolated values at $t_{1}$ and
$t_{2}$. Realizations will have different start and end times due to
the Bayesian age modeling process, so there are not necessarily 1000
realizations at every timestep. This process allows us to incorporate
the first and second moments of the Bayesian age distribution into our
inverse framework, and assumes unimodality in the covariance
distribution. This assumption is supported by the usage of a gamma distribution for the sediment accumulation prior \cite[]{Bacon}.

In addition, the $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ measurements
have a laboratory uncertainty of 0.07$\permil$
\cite{Lu-Oppo-2023:Surface}, which we represent with a diagonal
covariance matrix, $\mathbf{C_{\mathrm{lab}}}$, with values of
0.07$^{2}\permil^{2}$. The total covariance matrix associated with the
measurements, $\mathbf{C_{yy}}$, is the sum of
$\mathbf{C_{\mathrm{age}}}$ and $\mathbf{C_{\mathrm{lab}}}$,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:sumC}
\mathbf{C_{yy}} = \mathbf{C_{\mathrm{age}}} + \mathbf{C_{\mathrm{lab}}}. 
\end{equation}

\section{Temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}$ correlation \label{sec:corr}}
Here, we determine a value for the enforced temporal correlation
between temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}$ using isotope-enabled
Community Earth System Model (iCESM1.2) Last Millennium Ensemble
(LME) output \cite[]{Brady-Stevenson-2019:Connected}. Temperature, salinity and
$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ are observed at a subsurface
site ($\sim$1500 m) at the grid cell nearest to the sediment core
locations. The $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ timeseries exhibits
step-function variability, that is not present in either the
temperature or salinity timeseries, and may complicate its
interpretation (Supplemental Figure \ref{supp-fig:cesmts}).

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centerline{\includegraphics[width = \textwidth]{cesm_scatter.png}}
\caption[]{\label{fig:td18O_cesm}Relationships between (\textbf{A})
  temperature and salinity, and (\textbf{B}) salinity and
  $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\textrm{seawater}}$ from 5-year averaged
  timeseries from an interior grid cell near the sediment core
  locations (51.6 $^{\circ}$N, 21.9$^{\circ}$W, 1500m) in realization 002 of
  the iCESM1.2 LME. The red line shows the linear least squares fit to
  the data in each plot, with the optimized equation of best fit and
  associated R$^{2}$ value listed in the upper left of each panel. In
  \textbf{B}, the red dashed line shows the global
  $S = 2 \delta^{18}\mathrm{O}$ mixing line \cite[]{Schmidt-1999:Error,
    Gebbie2006}. \label{fig:td18O}}
\end{figure}

Temperature and salinity have a linear relationship of $T = 5.3S$
($R^{2} = 0.444$). This value is near the density-compensation ratio
for this temperature, salinity, and pressure level
(5.39). Density-compensation is a common feature of mixed-layer
waters, especially in regions that experience strong vertical mixing
and deep mixed layers \cite[]{Rudnick-Martin-2002}. Therefore, the
similarity in values could imply that this interior point in iCESM is
recording variability related to density-compensated mixed-layer waters. In addition,
density-compensation has been observed in Labrador Sea Water (LSW) as
it spreads from its origin after an intense convection event
\cite[]{Yashayaev-Aken-2007:Transformation}. LSW is present at the mid-depth location used here, serving as an additional
explanation of the similarity between the iCESM temperature-salinity
ratio and the density-compensation ratio.

Salinity and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ at this location
in iCESM have a linear relationship of $S = 2.8 \delta^{18}\mathrm{O}$
(Figure \ref{fig:td18O_cesm}). \citeA[]{Schmidt-1999:Error} identified
a global mixing line of
$S = 2.02 \delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ based upon upper ocean
observations from the Geochemical Ocean Sections Study, which was used
in \cite{Gebbie2006}. However, spatial gradients in the
salinity-$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ relationship have been
shown to often be a poor predictor of temporal gradients
\cite[]{Schmidt-1998:Oxygen, Schmidt-LeGrande-2007:Water}. Using a
different isotope-enabled climate model,
\citeA{Schmidt-LeGrande-2007:Water} reported a pre-industrial value of
$S = 2.5 \delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ for a North Atlantic
surface grid cell in a coupled climate model, which is closer to, and
is most likely a better comparison to our value of
$S = 2.8 \delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$.

We can substitute the identified salinity relation
($S = 2.8\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$) into the temperature
equation ($T = 5.3S$) to form an equation for temperature from
$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$
($T = 14.8 \delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$). This value will be
used to enforce correlation between surface temperature and
$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$. However,
as noted in Supplemental Section \ref{supp-sec:oxygenvar}, there are
multiple ways to arrive at a correlation value, and multiple possible
correlation values. In Section \ref{sec:gammasens}, we develop a model
for the sensitivity of our estimated North Atlantic temperature change
to this correlation value.

\begin{abstract}
Paleoceanographic reconstructions of the past 2,000 years indicate a large-scale cooling in the last millennium, but significant uncertainty on the spatial pattern and timing still exists. This uncertainty is partially due to the inference of surface conditions from sparse, noisy, and near-surface marine proxies that can exhibit seasonal and habitat depth biases. 
Here, we infer spatially-varying surface changes from benthic sediment core records by back-propagation through an ocean circulation model, improving upon prior methodologies that inferred regional or pointwise surface evolution from subsurface records. We present a surface temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ history that fits a dataset of 11 high-resolution benthic $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ records within error and also supports previous independent estimates obtained from Common Era marine surface records. Our methodology accounts for the opposing effects of temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ on $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$, as well as their covarying behavior.
Our estimate is characterized by a Little Ice Age sea surface temperature cooling of 0.54 $\pm$ 0.21 K between 1140-1880 CE, which is within error of planktonic SST record compilations, and exceeds the North Atlantic SST cooling exhibited in the Last Millennium Reanalysis.
The usage of benthic sediment core records improves the detection of long-term climate signals relative to surface-record reconstructions, because benthics reflect the properties of abyssal waters that are not subject to high-energy near-surface variability and record more stable signals than paired planktonic records. 
Our reconstruction is characterized by maximum cooling in the Nordic Seas, which has been previously identified in planktonic records. \section*{Plain Language Summary}
Historical evidence, as well as climate proxy records, have suggested that there was a period of anomalously cool global mean temperature, sometime in the past 500 years, called the Little Ice Age. Past estimates of the oceanic cooling related to the Little Ice Age have been obtained from chemical analyses on the shells of single-celled organisms, called foraminifera, that dwell in the surface layer of the ocean, and are preserved in the sediment record when they die. However, these records only reflect oceanic conditions at the surface at a particular point in space. Here, we build on prior techniques to infer the oceanic conditions at the surface over a continuous spatial area by using chemical analyses on the shells of bottom-dwelling foraminifera. These foraminifera record climate signals with higher fidelity, as they are not affected by near-surface variability, but are instead affected by bottom waters, which are composed of waters sourced from large areas of the surface ocean. We use an ocean circulation model to relate the composition of waters at the sediment core sites to the surface ocean, and quantify how surface signals may be modified by ocean circulation. From this relationship, we can then infer surface water characteristics over broader swaths of the surface based on bottom-dwelling foraminifera records. We find that the Little Ice Age cooling was largest in the Nordic Seas, and had an average magnitude of 0.54 $\pm$ 0.21 K when averaged across the North Atlantic. The identified cooling may have been stronger north of Iceland, and weaker south of Iceland, a spatial pattern previously suggested to exist in surface-dwelling foraminiferal records. \section{Introduction}
The past 2,000 years, referred to as the Common Era (CE), is a critical period for understanding the response of the Earth's climate to various perturbations on timescales longer than that of the instrumental record. One frequently observed characteristic of the climate evolution of the CE is the Little Ice Age (LIA), a global cooling anomaly that occurred in the past millennium, characterized by anomalously cool sea surface temperatures \cite{ocean2k} as well as surface air temperatures \cite[]{Consortium--2013:Continental}. Understanding the spatiotemporal extent of the LIA is a key step in constraining the response of the Earth's climate system to the known natural perturbations, which include decreasing solar irradiance due to the precession of the equinoxes, variable solar activity \cite[]{lapointe2021}, and stratospheric sulfate injection from volcanic eruptions \cite[]{Buntgen-Arseneault-2020:Prominent}.

Inferring the evolution of the LIA from observations is a challenging task due to the
sparse and noisy nature of paleoceanographic data. Specifically, prior estimates of LIA sea surface temperature (SST) cooling rates have been inferred largely from planktonic foraminfera, which represent a single point in space and can be difficult to extrapolate to regional means. In addition, planktonic records are subject to seasonal and habitat biases that can distort the recorded signal. These complications have most likely caused the magnitude and timing of MCA-LIA transition cooling to differ among reconstructions, from an estimate of 0.2 $^{\circ}$C/kyr from 1200-1940 CE in the northeast North Atlantic \cite[]{Cunningham-Austin-2013:Reconstruction}, to 0.42 $^{\circ}$C/kyr from 800-1800 CE globally \cite{ocean2k}. 

%Previously, modeling studies have been used to examine the ocean dynamics related to the LIA, and have suggested a strong connection between volcanism, Atlantic convection, and global temperature trends \cite{Zhong-Miller-2010:Centennial, miller12, Lehner2013, schleussner2013}. The internal variability of the subpolar gyre has also been suggested to play a strong role in amplifying LIA cooling \cite[]{Moreno-Chamarro-Zanchettin-2016:Little}. However, the full magnitude of the LIA cannot be explained through coupled climate models \cite{ottobliesner16}, complicating efforts to understand the relationship between climatic perturbations and response. Overall, more robust, data-informed estimates of the spatiotemporal structure of oceanic change related to the LIA is required to confirm the magnitude of cooling and more fully understand the associated dynamics.

Previously, these records were used to demonstrate, via changepoint analysis, that the deep ocean LIA transition, as indicated by the benthic records, occurred at 1350 $\pm$ 50 CE, while the surface ocean LIA transition, as indicated by planktonic records, occurred over a longer time interval, ranging from 1100-1700 CE \cite[]{Lu-Oppo-2023:Surface}. For the circulation model, we apply the empirical ocean circulation model described in \citeA[]{Gebbie-Huybers-2012:mean}, which was previously used to estimate coherent regional temperature histories over the CE based on results from a compilation of marine surface records, observations from the 1870s HMS Challenger expedition, and modern hydrography \cite{gebbie19}. However, no subsurface data preceding 1870 was used in this estimate. In this work, we seek to further refine the estimate of North Atlantic surface temperature through the usage of benthic records which will reflect more stable climatic signals than the planktonic estimate used in \citeA{gebbie19}.

\section{Methods}
The methodology outlined here quantifies the relationship between time-varying surface behavior, modified and propagated by circulation to depth where it is ``observed'' by sediment core sites. We begin in Section \ref{sec:spatmode} by identifying surface spatial modes that are relevant to the waters at the sediment core sites via singular value decomposition. These surface spatial modes are propagated to depth via the transient circulation model in Section \ref{sec:prop}. In Section \ref{sec:agemodel} we form our observation vector $\mathbf{y}$. A model matrix that maps between the magnitude of the surface spatial modes and the observations at the core sites is formed in Section \ref{sec:E}, and our solution technique is presented in Section \ref{sec:solution}. 

\subsection{Surface spatial modes \label{sec:spatmode}}
Similar to prior inverse problems, the underdetermined nature of this problem is a significant challenge. At face value, there are a high number of degrees of freedom present in a problem in which behavior about the surface ocean is inferred from deep sediment cores. However, we are able to take advantage of the large spatial scales relevant to atmospheric and surface ocean dynamics, and seek a spatially smooth solution, which helps to reduce the number of degrees of freedom. In addition, for our specific problem which relies on data from high-resolution North Atlantic sediment core, we seek a solution that prioritizes surface regions that contribute more water towards the sediment core sites. In order to achieve these goals, we utilize an approach in which we identify orthonormal surface spatial modes based on the singular value decomposition of surface water origination maps for each sediment core, obtained via a global circulation model (see Supplemental Section \ref{supp-sec:spatmode} for more details).

Modes are shown in Figure \ref{fig:svdmodes}, where the first mode explains $\approx$99\% of the variability in source water origination maps ($\mathbf{\Sigma}_{m}\sigma_{m}^{2}$) for all core sites. Mode 1 characterizes the general surface region that is most important to the sediment core sites, and consists of a single-signed signal with maximum magnitude located directly north of Iceland. This signal is strong across most of the Norwegian and Iceland Seas, and slightly weaker, but still present, in the Irminger Sea. Mode 2, which explains $\approx$1\% of the variability in source water origination maps for all core sites, is characterized by a signal centered in the Nordic Seas, and an anti-phased signal in the Labrador and Irminger Seas. This implies that source water origination maps for each core site tend to have an anti-phased contribution between the Nordic Seas and the Labrador and Irminger Seas (e.g., a sediment core location that has more water originating from the Nordic Seas will have less water originating from the Labrador and Irminger Seas, and vice versa). This relationship is expected from the hydrography of the region, as demonstrated by \citeA{Lu-Oppo-2023:Surface}. 

Although Mode 1 explains almost all of the variability in the surface origination maps, this does not necessarily imply that it is the only surface mode needed to explain the sediment core data. In Figure S\ref{supp-fig:U}, we show the left singular vectors, or unscaled principal components, of the surface source water origination maps, and note that Mode 1 has a consistently-valued negative contribution towards every sediment core site. This implies that we cannot explain any depth-dependent variability using solely Mode 1. In addition, Mode 1 cannot be used to explain a signal that arrives at different sediment cores with different timings. In Figure \ref{fig:recon}, records from sediment cores in intermediate waters (MC28A, MC25A) exhibit less of a positive trend in $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ than sediment cores in deep waters (MC13A, MC14A). In addition, we note consistent centennial-scale variability in sediment cores MC21A, MC20A, and MC19A. In order to explain these features, which are only present in certain depth ranges, there must be free parameters that have a depth-dependent effect upon misfit to the sediment core records. However, features highlighted here are near the magnitude of the uncertainty on the sediment core records, implying that they may not need to be perfectly fit in order to explain the data. This approach minimizes the degrees of freedom in the problem, while maximizing the amount of information our optimized estimate contains by quantifying the spatially-varying contribution of the surface ocean to the sediment core sites.

\subsection{Propagating surface spatial modes \label{sec:prop}}
In \citeA[]{Gebbie-Huybers-2012:mean}, the results of \citeA{gebbie2010} is extended by incorporating radiocarbon observations, resulting in a discrete time tracer transport operator $\mathbf{L}$ which is similar to $\mathbf{A}$, but represents mass fluxes instead of fractions. Specifically, the matrix $\mathbf{L}$ can be used to compute the time evolution of a three-dimensional tracer field $\mathbf{c}$, as
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:diffeq}
\mathbf{c}(t + \Delta t) = \mathbf{Lc}(t) + \mathbf{Bq}(t), 
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{B}$ is a Dirichlet boundary condition mapping matrix, and $\mathbf{q}(t)$ is a prescribed surface boundary condition that can be time-varying. 
Following the approach in \citeA[]{Gebbie-2012:Tracer}, we will propagate each surface spatial mode $\mathbf{v}_m$ through the global circulation model and compute the interpolated response at the sediment core sites. We set $\mathbf{q}(t)$ equal to a surface spatial mode $\mathbf{v}_{m}$ at all times, and then solve Equation (\ref{eq:diffeq}) for the propagated value of the surface spatial mode $\mathbf{c}_m$ over a period of time that exceeds the greatest expected transport timescale, at a resolution that smoothly captures the signal propagation. Here, we choose to solve for $\mathbf{c}_m(\tau)$ starting from 1 year post-signal, to 2000 years post-signal, at 1 year resolution with a quasi-constant timestep numerical differentiation function (QNDF) solver \cite[]{doi:10.1137/S1064827594276424}. At each time step, we save the interpolated value of $\mathbf{c}_{m}(\tau)$ at each core site. This process is equivalent to computing the step response to a constant-in-time boundary condition, which is much more computationally efficient than computing the response to a time-varying boundary condition. The impulse response function, or the response to a surface condition that persists for 1 year, and then disappears, can be computed by taking the time derivative of the step response, which we accomplish with a center-difference scheme.

We compile all impulse response functions for each sediment core $c$, surface spatial mode $m$, at all lagged times $\tau$ into the array $\bm{\mathcal{M}}$. We can consider this array to characterize the effect for any lagged time at any core site from an impulse signal of the form of a surface spatial mode $\mathbf{v}_m$. As an example, in Supplemental Figure S\ref{supp-fig:modemags}, we note that the impulse response function of Mode 1 has nearly identical evolution at the sediment core sites, which is accordance with our prior observation that it has similar power over all sediment core sites. The impulse response function of Mode 2 demonstrates that the positive-signed anomaly north of Iceland in Mode 2 is more important for deep sediment cores, while the negative-signed anomaly south of Iceland affects the intermediate sediment core sites. This further reinforces our choice to not truncate any modes, as the modes which explain a low percentage of the variance in surface source water origination maps can be used to explain depth-varying behavior at the sediment core sites.

\subsection{Assembling observations \label{sec:agemodel}}
In this inversion, we are solving for the evolution of surface properties that best explain the collective behavior of a set of 11 high-resolution benthic foraminiferal $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ sediment core records previously described in \citeA[]{Lu-Oppo-2023:Surface}. These records were collected from along the Reykjanes Ridge offshore of Iceland, and form a depth transect from 1100-2300m. The $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ measurements were obtained from \textit{Uvigerina peregrina} (\textit{U. peregrina}) tests for the three shallowest cores (MC28A, MC26A, and MC25A), and from \textit{Cibicidoides wuellerstorfi} (\textit{C. wuellerstorfi}) tests for the other eight sediment cores.

The measured $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ of a foraminiferal test is dependent upon the ambient temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ at the time of calcification \cite[]{Urey-1947, McCrea-1950:Isotopic}. The $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ is in turn affected by equilibrium fractionation processes such that higher $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ is associated with saltier waters, while lower $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ is associated with fresher waters \cite[]{Emiliani--1955:Pleistocene, Bemis-Spero-1998:Reevaluation}. Ultimately, higher values of $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ are associated with either cooler temperatures and/or more saline waters. \citeA{marchitto} established the linear approximation,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:cibs}
\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}} = \alpha T + \beta + (\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}} - 0.27\permil),
\end{equation}
where $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ is in $\permil$ Pee Dee Belemnite (PDB) and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ is in $\permil$ Standard Mean Ocean Water (SMOW). For \textit{C. wuellerstorfi}, $\alpha = -0.224 \pm 0.002$ $\permil\mathrm{ K}^{-1}$ and $\beta = 3.53 \pm 0.02\permil$. While \textit{C. wuellerstorfi} calcifies at equilibrium, \textit{U. peregrina} is isotopically heavier by 0.47$\permil$ \cite[]{marchitto}. In this work, we follow the recommendation of \citeA{marchitto} to remove the equilibration offset from \textit{U. peregrina} records, and apply Equation (\ref{eq:cibs}).

In \citeA{Lu-Oppo-2023:Surface}, the Bacon software package in R \cite{Bacon, McKay-Emile-Geay-2021:R} was used to generate a Bayesian accumulation history for each sediment core based on 3-8 radiocarbon samples and an assigned coretop age (see \citeA{Lu-Oppo-2023:Surface} for more details). Each sediment core has an age model that consists of 1000 realizations of age with respect to depth. The $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ series, which is originally recorded as a function of depth within the sediment core, is aligned to each age model realization, providing an ensemble of $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ measurements with varying age evolution. Here, we incorporate the first and second moments of the age model ensemble by sampling the $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ ensemble to produce a distribution of possible $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ values at any given time. These distributions can be used to compute a best estimate of $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$, as well as compute the $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ covariance at any two times, detailed in Supplemental Section \ref{supp-sec:agemodel}. 

Once the observational vector $\mathbf{y}$ is formed, ensuring that it can be interpreted in the context of modern-day records is key. We find that the depth structure in recorded CE $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ is offset from modern-day estimates of $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$, especially at shallower sediment cores (Figure \ref{fig:offset}). The variable offset between the observed $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ and estimated modern-day effective $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ implies that, if modern-day observations were used to construct the prior, the inversion would be minimizing the misfit to the depth structure, not to the CE signal at each sediment core site. Therefore, due to conflicting values in the equilibration offset of \textit{U. peregrina} \cite[]{Shackleton1974AttainmentOI, marchitto}, as well as uncertainty in realistic modern day estimates of $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ at depth, we choose to work in anomaly space. This allows us to ensure that we fit the CE signal recorded in each sediment core instead of the depth structure of $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$, which we have demonstrated is difficult to quantitatively interpret. In order to convert $\mathbf{y}$ into anomaly space, we subtract the mean interpolated values between 1850-1980 CE from each record.

\subsection{Model Equation \label{sec:E}}
We relate the signals observed at deep sediment core sites to the preceding conditions at the sea surface, accounting for the modification of those signals by ocean circulation, as well as the timescale of their propagation. We decompose the surface into distinct spatial patterns, and solve for the magnitude of these patterns for both temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$. As a physical model equation, we can represent this as a convolution over the relevant state variables $s$ and lagged times $\tau$, 
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:impresp}
y_{t}^c = \sum_s \sum_{\tau} \alpha^s \bm{u}_{t - \tau}^s \cdot \bm{\mathcal{M}}_\tau ^c, 
\end{equation}
where $y_{t}^{c}$ is a property observed at a sediment core $c$ and time $t$, $\alpha^{s}$ is a scaling factor associated with the state variable $s$, $\bm{u}_{t - \tau}^s$ is a vector of all spatial mode magnitudes at time $t-\tau$ and for state variable $s$, and $\bm{\mathcal{M}}_\tau ^c$ is a vector containing the propagated magnitude of all modes at a lagged time $\tau$, and observed at sediment core $c$. This model equation combines both the circulation and proxy system model into one equation.

Here, the observation vector $\mathbf{y}$ consists of $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ measurements, which are a function of the mode magnitudes of temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ in the state vector $\mathbf{u}$. As these variables are largely conservative in the interior ocean \cite[]{Lynch-Stieglitz-2003:Tracers}, we treat them as passive tracers, and assume linearity. The relationship between the observation vector, $\mathbf{y}$, and the solution vector, $\mathbf{u}$, is characterized by the model matrix, $\mathbf{E}$, and is obtained via the discrete time transport operator $\mathbf{L}$ from \citeA{Gebbie-Huybers-2012:mean}. The usage of $\mathbf{L}$ allows us to constrain the effect of any surface signal on the sediment core sites as a function of time lag from some initial perturbation. This results in the linear inverse problem, 
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:inverse}
\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{Eu}. 
\end{equation}
Individual rows of the model matrix $\mathbf{E}$ from Equation (\ref{eq:inverse}) can be identified by making small perturbations to an arbitrary state vector, $\mathbf{u}$, applying Equation (\ref{eq:impresp}), and scaling the output by the perturbation.

\subsection{Solution method \label{sec:solution}}
We solve Equation (\ref{eq:inverse}) for the time-varying magnitudes of surface patterns, $\mathbf{u}$, by using the Gauss-Markov Theorem. This methodology minimizes solution uncertainty, as defined according to \citeA[]{Wunsch-2006:Discrete}, through the cost function, 
\begin{equation}  
\label{eq:costfunc}
\tilde{J} = \left(\mathbf{y}-\mathbf{\tilde{y}}\right)^T\mathbf{C_{yy}}^{-1}\left(\mathbf{y}-\mathbf{\tilde{y}}\right)+(\mathbf{\tilde{u} - u_0})^T\mathbf{C_{u_{0}u_{0}}}^{-1}(\mathbf{\tilde{u} - u_0}), 
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{\tilde{u}}$ represents values of $\mathbf{u}$ that best minimize $\tilde{J}$ and $\mathbf{u_0}$ is the prior, or first guess, of $\mathbf{\tilde{u}}$. The vector $\mathbf{\tilde{y}}$ is the estimated values at the core sites according to $\mathbf{\tilde{u}}$ (e.g. $\mathbf{\tilde{y}} = \mathbf{E\tilde{u}}$). %Finally, the matrices $\mathbf{C_{yy}}$ and $\mathbf{C_{u_0u_0}}$ are covariance matrices defining the expected statistics of $\mathbf{y}$ and $\mathbf{u_0}$, respectively.

We solve for the value of $\mathbf{\tilde{u}}$ that minimizes $d\tilde{J}$/$d\mathbf{\tilde{u}}$,  %$\frac{d\tilde{J}}{d\mathbf{\tilde{u}}}$, 
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{\tilde{u}} = \mathbf{u_{0}} + \mathbf{C_{u_{0}u_{0}}} \mathbf{E}^T\left(\mathbf{E}\mathbf{C_{u_{0}u_{0}}}\mathbf{E}^T + \mathbf{C_{yy}}\right)^{-1}\left(\mathbf{y}-\mathbf{Eu_0}\right)
\label{eq:twls}.
\end{equation}
The associated covariance can be computed according to
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{C_{\tilde{u}\tilde{u}}} = \mathbf{C_{u_0u_0}}-\mathbf{C_{u_0u_0}}\mathbf{E}^T(\mathbf{E}\mathbf{C_{u_0u_0}}\mathbf{E}^T + \mathbf{C_{yy}}) \mathbf{E}\mathbf{C_{u_0u_0}}. 
    \label{eq:twlsCuu}
    \end{equation}

In summary, we have proposed a methodology that produces the temporal evolution of distinct surface modes of variability that best explain benthic sediment core data. This methodology propagates a full representation of the first and second statistical moments of the observations throughout the problem. For the first time, we simultaneously account for factors such as the age model uncertainty, the competing effects of temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ upon $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$, and the modification and lag of surface signals due to ocean circulation. 
\section{Results}
Here, we present two estimates of temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$, differentiated by their temporal extent and observational constraints. For each estimate, the included sediment core records are trimmed to the common time period shared by all sediment cores in order to maintain numerical stability. We refer to the estimate that includes all 11 records in the \citeA[]{Lu-Oppo-2023:Surface} dataset as the \texttt{OPT-11} estimate, which is constrained by data from 1470-1980 CE. This estimate does not cover the beginning of the LIA, and is therefore not suitable for estimating quantities such as the magnitude of LIA cooling. In order to capture the onset of the LIA, we present the \texttt{OPT-3} estimate, which fits sediment cores MC26A, MC22A, and MC13A. These three sediment cores, which have the largest temporal extent (800-1980 CE) within this dataset, also sample intermediate (MC26A: 1031m), deep (MC22A: 1535m), and abyssal (MC13A: 2129m) waters. This estimate is suitable for assessing the magnitude of MCA-LIA cooling, but is not expected to fit all 11 sediment cores as well as \texttt{OPT-11}.

The relationship between the optimized mode magnitudes and observations is defined via the impulse response functions of each surface spatial mode interpolated at each sediment core sites, which we show examples of in Supplemental Figure S\ref{supp-fig:modemags}. Most impulse response functions have initial signal peaks between 10-25 years from the initial signal, and return to null values by 100 years. This means that an observation, at some arbitrary timestep, will have the most power over the predicted surface spatial mode magnitude 10-25 years before that arbitrary timestep, and will have no power over the predicted surface spatial mode magnitude prior to 100 years pre-signal. This explains some relevant numerical features of the optimized mode magnitudes in Figure \ref{fig:utilde}, such as the unconstrained values (i.e., $\mathbf{\tilde{u}} = 0$) before the existence of data in $\mathbf{y}$. The mode magnitude vector, $\mathbf{u}$, is defined over a longer time period than the observational vector, $\mathbf{y}$, because of the lagged response of signals at the core sites to surface conditions. However, at some time preceding the last observational timestep in $\mathbf{y}$, the observations will no longer have any power to predict past surface behavior, and the estimated mode magnitude, $\mathbf{\tilde{u}}$, will revert to the prior, $\mathbf{u_0}$. Another important, and related, numerical feature is that the optimized value at the final timestep also reverts to the prior ($\mathbf{\tilde{u}} = 0$). This occurs because most of the information relevant to determining $\mathbf{u}$ at the final timestep has not had long enough to propagate to depth.

Both estimates fall within the 1$\sigma$ error of the original sediment core records, confirming that the data is well fit. Centennial-scale variability, such as that observed in sediment cores MC21A, MC20A, and MC19A, is not captured in either reconstructed estimate, but this does not appear to contribute significantly to the cost function value, most likely due to the magnitude of observational uncertainty. The \texttt{OPT-11} estimate fits all sediment cores slightly better than \texttt{OPT-3}, especially for the sediment cores that are not observational constraints on \texttt{OPT-3}. We interpret \texttt{OPT-11} as a more data-informed result for the past 500 years, whereas \texttt{OPT-3} is informed by less data, but extends for over 1000 years. In Figure \ref{fig:utilde}, a more sustained period of peak Little Ice Age cooling was noted in \texttt{OPT-11} than in \texttt{OPT-3}. The sediment core reconstruction shows that sediment cores MC21A, MC20A, MC10A, and MC9A (which are all constraints on \texttt{OPT-11} but not \texttt{OPT-3}) exhibit this more sustained Little Ice Age, hence the difference between the two estimates.

Although many of the sediment core records in close proximity have disparate mean signals (i.e., MC19A and MC10A), our estimates are a fairly ``middle-of-the-road'' compromise between all signals, in which the reconstruction is fairly similar between neighboring core sites and seeks to optimize a common signal from all sediment core records. This result is in accordance with Mode 1 being the only constrained mode, as it is a single-signed surface anomaly with a similar time evolution across all sediment cores sites. 
\subsection{Surface reconstructions \label{sec:surfrecon}}
Output surface maps of temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ throughout time can be constructed by propagating spatial mode magnitudes, at a specific time, $t_u$, and for a specific state variable, $s$, through the $\mathbf{V}$ matrix, 
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:surfrecon}
  \mathbf{\tilde{s}}[t_u, s] = \mathbf{V \tilde{u}}[t_u, s]. 
\end{equation}
The associated covariance matrix will be
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:surfreconunc}
\mathbf{C_{\tilde{s}\tilde{s}}}[t_u, s] = \mathbf{V C_{\tilde{u}\tilde{u}}}[t_u, s]\mathbf{V}^T.
\end{equation}

At this point, a question arises out of the discovery that Mode 1 alone can suitably explain the sediment core records: would other North Atlantic surface patterns also be able to explain the sediment core records? If not, what are the important characteristics of a surface pattern that could solely explain the signals recorded in these sediment cores? In order to answer this question, we generate an estimate, in which, instead of providing 11 distinct spatial modes with which to fit the data, we only provide one globally uniform mode. This estimate is referred to as \texttt{OPT-3-GLOBAL}, and we find that it fits the observations similarly to \texttt{OPT-3} (6.28 for \texttt{OPT-3}, 13.1 for \texttt{OPT-3-GLOBAL}, with an expected value of 357 for both estimates). This is further illustrated in Figure \ref{fig:reconalt}, where we plot the reconstructed estimate at the sediment core sites, and show a similar fit to the records as \texttt{OPT-3} in Figure \ref{fig:recon}. One caveat is that, because the spatial mode does not increase in power over the regions of the North Atlantic that contribute the most waters to the core site, the magnitude of MCA-LIA cooling must be very high globally ($\sim$0.7 K) in order to fit the sediment core data. As a result of this very high MCA-LIA cooling, this solution seems less likely than the \texttt{OPT-3} result, despite fitting the sediment core records well.

To illustrate if any North Atlantic pattern would be equally as skillful at fitting the sediment core observations, we generate an estimate using solely Mode 2. The Mode 2 spatial pattern is characterized by a negative anomaly south of Iceland, and a positive anomaly north of Iceland. This estimate is referred to as \texttt{OPT-3-MODE2}, and exhibits a worse fit to the observational cost function (89.9) than either \texttt{OPT-3} and \texttt{OPT-3-GLOBAL}, although still less than the expected value. Specifically, \texttt{OPT-3-MODE2} has a much worse fit to sediment core MC13A than \texttt{OPT-3} and \texttt{OPT-3-GLOBAL}. The impulse response functions of the globally uniform signal (Supplemental Figure S\ref{supp-fig:impulse}) bear similarity to the Mode 1 impulse response functions, with a similar time evolution of the signal at all three sediment core sites. Contrarily, the Mode 2 impulse response functions have very different time evolution, in which the MC26A and MC25A functions respond to the negative anomaly in Mode 2, while the MC13A function responds to the positive anomaly in Mode 2. Therefore, we conclude that, in order to fit the sediment core data appropriately, we require a surface spatial mode that is associated with very similar impulse response functions at all sediment core sites.

As demonstrated via \texttt{OPT-3-GLOBAL}, the Mode 1 surface spatial pattern is not the only surface spatial pattern that can fit the sediment core records. With the \texttt{OPT-3-GLOBAL} estimate, we generated a fit to the sediment core records that was on par with \texttt{OPT-3}. However, \texttt{OPT-3-GLOBAL} reconstructed a global cooling that exceeded many other estimates. Through the \texttt{OPT-3-MODE2} estimate, we showed that the sediment core data cannot be fit by any arbitrary pattern. Our sediment core signals are best fit by a surface pattern that has a similar propagation signal at the sediment core sites, which not all surface patterns will have, due to the timescales of ocean circulation. This is a result of the common signal of MCA-LIA cooling recorded at all sediment core sites, which has fairly similar magnitude across all depths, and thus requires a surface pattern that affects all sediment cores synchronously and equally. Although the sediment core records exhibit core-to-core deviations, a common signal is able to fit all records within error. From solely this analysis, there is no way to infer from the benthic sediment core records alone if the globally-uniform pattern, or the Mode 1 pattern, is more likely to have been the actual pattern of MCA-LIA cooling. However, as exhibited in Figure \ref{fig:ocean2kcomp}, the spatial pattern of Mode 1 is replicated by the rate of cooling in planktonic Mg/Ca records, as previously discussed by \citeA[]{Sicre-Hall-2011:Sea}. 

\subsection{Reconstruction at sediment core sites}
The \texttt{OPT-3} and \texttt{OPT-11} solutions both agree, within uncertainties, with the sediment core records. However, the two estimates have different time evolution, with \texttt{OPT-3} cooling until the beginning of the 20th century, and \texttt{OPT-11} exhibiting a sustained LIA that lasted from the mid-1500s until the beginning of the 20th century. \texttt{OPT-11} represents a slightly better fit to all sediment core records, and for that reason is our preferred estimate post-1500. Surface proxy records have not reached any consensus on the timing or evolution of the MCA-LIA transition, making it difficult to validate either estimate in this regard \cite[]{MoffaSanchez:Variability}.

\subsection{Surface reconstructions}
In this work, we have presented a new estimate of LIA surface cooling inferred from benthic sediment core records. Our reconstruction suggests a fairly high magnitude cooling during the last millennium (-0.71 $\pm$ 0.28 K/kyr). \citeA[]{Cunningham-Austin-2013:Reconstruction} reconstructed SST for the northeast North Atlantic using a composite plus regression approach to integrate planktonic foraminiferal records, estimating a MCA-LIA cooling of 0.2 $^{\circ}$C/kyr that appeared after 1250 CE and persisted until the early 20th Century. As previously stated, \citeA{ocean2k} report a global mean SST cooling of 0.42 $^{\circ}$C/kyr, which lies almost exactly on the -1$\sigma$ line of our North Atlantic reconstruction. Terrestrial cooling in the surrounding regions has been estimated to be 0.47 $^{\circ}$C/kyr in the Arctic, 0.09$^{\circ}$C/kyr in Europe, and 0.34 $^{\circ}$C/kyr in North America \cite[]{Consortium--2013:Continental}. This mismatch in terrestrial and marine cooling rates may be a result of seasonal biases in proxies. In particular, the bottom waters that our benthic foraminifera record are seasonally biased towards wintertime conditions \cite[]{Stommel-1979:Determination, Williams-Marshall-1995:Does}. Therefore, we interpret this result as evidence for temperature changes in the regions of deepwater formation that outpace reconstructed terrestrial MCA-LIA temperature changes, for all regions besides the Arctic.

In Figure \ref{fig:meants}, we noted considerable disagreement between the LMR and our reconstructions. The LMR is an offline data assimilation product, which uses CMIP models to construct a prior and constrains it through the PAGES2k database \cite[]{Hakim-Emile-Geay-2016:last}. This disagreement could have several causes, such as seasonal biases affecting both terrestrial and marine proxies, as well as model biases related to North Atlantic ocean dynamics \cite[]{Heuze2017} that may affect the LMR prior.

In \citeA{gebbie19}, peak North Atlantic LIA was identified in the 1800s, which was noted to be amplified from the global average. This feature was suggested to be due to a winter-amplified MCA-LIA cooling, preferentially recorded through deepwater formation. The North Atlantic reconstruction of \citeA{gebbie19} falls mostly within the 1$\sigma$ error of both OPT-3 and OPT-11, although it displays more centennial-scale variability that is not present in our reconstruction. In addition, all surface region temperature reconstructions of \citeA{gebbie19} exhibit cooling beginning prior to 1000 CE, while our reconstruction, and the Ocean2k synthesis, appear to have a later cooling onset. However, as shown in Figure \ref{fig:plankticstack}, the planktic stack from \citeA{Lu-Oppo-2023:Surface} may indicate an earlier onset LIA. Although this disparity warrants more investigation, it suggests that, in order for the interior ocean to match the Challenger - WOCE warming pattern, and fit the Ocean2k cooling magnitude, an earlier LIA is warranted.

One physical explanation for the higher magnitude cooling observed in the North Atlantic relative to non-Arctic land masses could be the amplification of Arctic temperature change. Arctic Holocene cooling has been suggested to be the result of a decrease in summer insolation, which was stronger at higher latitudes, that was subsequently reinforced by sea-ice and terrestrial snow feedback mechanisms\cite[]{Kaufman2009}. \citeA{Miller-Alley-2010:Arctic} used a variety of summer paleoclimate syntheses to demonstrate Arctic amplification of 3-4 times the global mean temperature during the Holocene Thermal Maximum, Last Glacial Maximum, and mid-Pliocene. Although amplified Arctic warming of up to four times the magnitude of the global mean signal has been identified in observations since 1980 \cite[]{Park-Kim-2019:Mid}, Arctic amplification has not been clearly identifiable during the entirety of the instrumental record \cite[]{England-Eisenman-2021:Arctic}. This has been attributed to the cancellation of greenhouse gas warming by anthropogenic aerosol release, as well as sensitivity to the phase of internal variability during periods of forcing \cite[]{England-Eisenman-2021:Arctic}. Arctic amplification of the MCA-LIA cooling has been identified in CESM \cite{ottobliesner16}, but has been difficult to identify in paleoclimate records. 

Limitations of this methodology include the enforced correlation of 10 K/$\permil$ between temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$. In the future, independent constraints on temperature, such as those obtained from Mg/Ca records, could be used to remove this constraint. In addition, constraining the expected variance of spatial mode magnitudes from CESM output implies that we are seeking out solutions that have similar ocean variability to the simulated historical (1850-2005) ocean. Therefore, our solutions should be interpreted as possible solutions for surface histories of $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ and temperature as expected with similar climatologies. This does not exclude the possibility of a reality in which these relationships are different, but suggests that this dataset does not necessitate changes in these relationships. 

There are many avenues for expanding the results presented here, such as expanding the included proxy records. This could be accomplished by incorporating more benthic $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ records, such as the compilation of \citeA[]{Lund-Curry-2006:Florida}, but could also include adapting the methodology to include constraints based on surface records. Although North Atlantic CE surface records have exhibited significant disagreement, qualitative regional patterns such as a East and South Greenland millennial-scale cooling have been identified \cite[]{MoffaSanchez:Variability}, and could be further confirmed through an adaption of the presented methodology. In addition, historical ship-based measurements could be included to refine the industrial warming estimates, as was presented in \citeA{gebbie19}.

\section{Conclusion}
Here, we have demonstrated a novel methodology of inferring surface climate conditions from benthic sediment core records via an ocean circulation model, while accounting for the lag in transit time from the surface to depth, age model uncertainty, and the unequal contributions of temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ towards $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$. In addition, dimension reduction via a source water decomposition was utilized to increase the computational tractability of the problem. The effects of temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ were separated via established experimental relations and correlations from climate model output. This methodology resulted in the first 1000-year reconstruction of sea surface temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ from benthic sediment core records.

We generated two estimates of Common Era North Atlantic temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$, \texttt{OPT-3}, which spanned the end of the MCA and the onset of the LIA, and \texttt{OPT-11}, which captured only the peak of the LIA and the subsequent recovery toward industrial conditions. \texttt{OPT-3} was only constrained by three sediment core records, but extended from 800-1970 CE, while \texttt{OPT-11} was constrained by 11 sediment core records, but only extended from 1470-1980 CE. Both estimates fit the sediment core records within error, with the main difference being that \texttt{OPT-11} reconstructed a sustained LIA, while \texttt{OPT-3} reconstructed a MCA-LIA cooling that persisted until the early 20th century. We interpret the sustained LIA of the \texttt{OPT-11} estimate as being the more likely solution for the post-1470 CE, but the \texttt{OPT-3} solution is still suitable for estimating the rate of MCA-LIA cooling (0.71 $\pm$ 0.28 K/kyr).

We found that, in both estimates, the data could be well fit by using the primary mode of surface water origination at the sediment core sites, referred to here as Mode 1, which was characterized by a strong anomaly north of Iceland. Although other modes of variability, such as a uniform surface pattern, are able to fit the data, the Mode 1 spatial pattern was the only investigated mode that both fit the sediment core data, and generated temperature reconstructions that were in agreement with independent reconstructions. This spatial pattern of cooling was also observed in planktonic SST records in the region of interest. 

Our results provide evidence for a Nordic Sea-centered MCA-LIA cooling with a magnitude that agrees with recent marine proxy compilations \cite{ocean2k} and Arctic terrestrial cooling estimates \cite{Consortium--2013:Continental}. This reconstructed cooling outweighs some marine planktonic reconstructions \cite[]{Cunningham-Austin-2013:Reconstruction}, as well as estimates of North American and European terrestrial cooling \cite{Consortium--2013:Continental}. We interpret this discrepancy as evidence for a MCA-LIA strengthened cooling in regions of deepwater formation, and suggestive of the Arctic amplification of MCA-LIA cooling.

\begin{abstract}
Paleoceanographic reconstructions of the past 2,000 years indicate a large-scale cooling in the latter half of the last millennium, with significant uncertainty on the spatial pattern and timing. This uncertainty is partially due to the inference of surface conditions from sparse, noisy, near-surface marine proxies that can exhibit seasonal biases.
Here, we extend prior methodologies to infer spatially-varying surface changes from benthic sediment core records by back-propagation through an ocean circulation model. The usage of benthic sediment core records represents an improvement on past reconstruction techniques, because they reflect the properties of abyssal waters that are not subject to high-energy near-surface variability and record more stable signals than paired planktonic records. 
We present a surface temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ history that fits a dataset of 11 high-resolution benthic $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ records within error and also confirms previous independent estimates obtained from Common Era marine surface records. 
Our estimate is characterized by a Little Ice Age sea surface temperature cooling of 0.71 $\pm$ 0.28 K/kyr between 1140-1900 CE, which is within the error of planktonic SST record compilations, and exceeds the cooling exhibited in the Last Millennium Reanalysis.
We account for the opposing effects and covarying behavior of temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ on $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$, resulting in higher estimates of temperature change. 
Our reconstruction is explained by a single surface mode, which is also reflected in three, independent, regional SST records. This spatial pattern of cooling is characterized by a larger magnitude cooling in the Nordic Seas than in the subpolar gyre, a pattern that has previously been associated with the southward migration of the polar front. 
\end{abstract}

\section*{Plain Language Summary}
Historical evidence, as well as climate proxy records, have suggested that there was a period of anomalously cool temperatures, sometime in the past 500 years, called the Little Ice Age. However, it has been challenging to understand the exact timing and spatial extent of this cool period. This is partially due to the fact that the Little Ice Age is thought to be related to North Atlantic ocean dynamics, and there are limited observations from this period. Past estimates of the oceanic cooling related to the Little Ice Age have been obtained from chemical analyses on the shells of single-celled organisms, called foraminifera, that dwell in the surface layer of the ocean, and are preserved in the sediment record when they die. However, these records only reflect oceanic conditions at the surface at a particular point in space. Here, we build on prior techniques to infer the oceanic conditions at the surface over a much larger spatial area. This is accomplished by using chemical analyses on the shells of bottom-dwelling foraminifera. Bottom-dwelling foraminifera record clearer climate signals, as they are not affected by near-surface variability, such as the seasonal cycle. The bottom waters that affect these organisms are composed of waters sourced from large areas of the surface ocean. We use an ocean circulation model to relate the composition of waters at the sediment core sites to the surface ocean. From this relationship, we can then infer surface water characteristics over broader swathes of sthe surface, based on the bottom-dwelling foraminifera records. We find Little Ice Age cooling was largest in the Nordic Seas, and had an average magnitude of 0.71 $\pm$ 0.28 K/kyr when averaged across the North Atlantic. We find that the cooling may have been stronger north of Iceland, and weaker south of Iceland, a spatial pattern previously suggested to exist in surface-dwelling foraminiferal records. This pattern has been proposed to be related to an advance of polar waters.

\section{Introduction}
The past 2,000 years, referred to as the Common Era (CE), is a critical period for understanding the response of the Earth's climate to various perturbations on timescales longer than that of the instrumental record. One frequently observed characteristic of the climate evolution of the CE is the Little Ice Age (LIA), a global cooling anomaly that occurred in the past millennium, characterized by anomalously cool sea surface temperatures \cite{ocean2k} as well as surface air temperatures \cite[]{Consortium--2013:Continental}. Understanding the spatiotemporal extent of the LIA is a key step in constraining the response of the Earth's climate system to the known natural perturbations, which include decreasing solar irradiance due to the precession of the equinoxes, variable solar activity \cite[]{lapointe2021}, and stratospheric sulfate injection from volcanic eruptions \cite[]{Buntgen-Arseneault-2020:Prominent}. 

Inferring the evolution of the LIA from observations is a challenging task due to the point-wise, sparse, and noisy nature of paleoceanographic data. Estimates of LIA sea surface temperature (SST) cooling rates as well as timing differ among reconstructions, from an estimate of 0.2 $^{\circ}$C/kyr from 1200-1940 CE in the northeast North Atlantic \cite[]{Cunningham-Austin-2013:Reconstruction}, to 0.42 $^{\circ}$C/kyr from 800-1800 CE globally \cite{ocean2k}. These reconstructions all rely upon surface records, which experience a high degree of noise, aliasing, and seasonal effects. 

Previously, modeling studies have been used to examine the ocean dynamics related to the LIA, and have suggested a strong connection between volcanism, Atlantic convection, and global temperature trends \cite{Zhong-Miller-2010:Centennial, miller12, Lehner2013, schleussner2013}. The internal variability of the subpolar gyre has also been suggested to play a strong role in amplifying LIA cooling \cite[]{Moreno-Chamarro-Zanchettin-2016:Little}. However, the full magnitude of the LIA cannot be explained through coupled climate models \cite{ottobliesner16}, complicating efforts to understand the relationship between climatic perturbations and response. Overall, more robust, data-informed estimates of the spatiotemporal structure of oceanic change related to the LIA is required to confirm the magnitude of cooling and more fully understand the associated dynamics.

%In addition, paleoceanographic proxy records have not identified large-scale circulation anomalies in the pre-industrial CE \cite{Thornalley-Oppo-2018:Anomalously, moffasanchez2015, Rahmstorf-Box-2015:Exceptional}. Dynamics related to a weakened subpolar gyre \cite{Moffa-Sanchez-Born-2014:Solar, Moreno-Chamarro-Zanchettin-2016:Little} have also been suggested to be a key reinforcement mechanism, and have been supported through deep-sea coral records \cite{Copard-Colin-2012:Late}, bivalve resiliency records \cite{Arellano-Nava2022}, and planktonic foraminiferal records \cite[]{Moffa-Sanchez-Born-2014:Solar, moffasanchez2017, Spooner-Thornalley-2020:Exceptional}, although exact timing and spatial extent of subpolar gyre variability still remain unclear. 

In this paper, we develop an inverse approach to infer surface properties from benthic proxy records through the usage of a data-informed circulation model. We apply a modern-day circulation model under the assumption that there were no large-scale circulation changes over the CE, as has been suggested by prior research \cite{Thornalley-Oppo-2018:Anomalously, moffasanchez2015, Rahmstorf-Box-2015:Exceptional}. The circulation model used here is a transient inversion of modern-day tracer observations \cite[]{Gebbie-Huybers-2012:mean} previously used to estimate coherent regional temperature histories that could explain the observed warming from the 1870s HMS Challenger expedition to modern hydrography \cite[]{gebbie19}. Here, we apply this model to obtain surface climate patterns that best fit CE sediment core benthic foraminiferal $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}$ records for the first time. We improve on prior methodologies \cite{Amrhein-Gebbie-2015:Inferring, gebbie19} by incorporating age model uncertainty into our estimates, separating the effects of temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$, and inferring the magnitude of spatially-varying modes instead of uniform surface regions or points, allowing us to generate smoothly-varying spatiotemporal maps. 

\section{Data}
\subsection{Sediment Core Records from Lu et al. (2023) \label{sec:ludata}}
In this inversion, we are solving for the evolution of surface properties that best explain the collective behavior of a set of 11 high-resolution benthic foraminiferal $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ sediment core records (Figure \ref{fig:recon}) previously described in \citeA[]{Lu-Oppo-2023:Surface}. These records were collected from along the Reykjanes Ridge offshore of Iceland, and form a depth transect from 1100-2300m. Previously, these records were used to demonstrate that the deep ocean LIA transition, as indicated by the benthic records, occurred at 1350 $\pm$ 50 CE, while the surface ocean LIA transition, as indicated by planktonic records, occurred over a longer time interval, ranging from 1100-1700 CE. In addition, a larger magnitude LIA was observed in the benthic records than in the planktonic records, suggested to indicate an Arctic amplification of LIA temperature changes, transferred to the sediment core sites by an active Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation \cite[]{Lu-Oppo-2023:Surface}.

\citeA{Lu-Oppo-2023:Surface} used two benthic species of foraminifera to measure $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$. The $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ measurements were obtained from \textit{Uvigerina peregrina} (\textit{U. peregrina}) tests for the three shallowest cores (MC28A, MC26A, and MC25A), and from \textit{Cibicidoides wuellerstorfi} (\textit{C. wuellerstorfi}) tests for the other eight sediment cores. The Bacon software package in R \cite{Bacon, McKay-Emile-Geay-2021:R} was used to generate a Bayesian accumulation history for each sediment core based on 3-8 radiocarbon samples and an assigned coretop age (see \citeA{Lu-Oppo-2023:Surface} for more details). Each sediment core has an age model that consists of 1000 realizations of age with respect to depth. 
  
\subsection{Total Matrix Intercomparison (TMI), steady-state and transient versions}
In order to relate signals at the sediment core sites to the surface, we apply an ocean circulation model. Specifically, we use the results of \citeA{gebbie2010}, in which a box inversion technique is used to compute the pathways connecting interior ocean grid boxes to the surface. The global ocean is decomposed into a $2^{\circ} \times 2^{\circ}$ grid with 33 vertical levels, and the pathways that connect each interior grid box to the surface are computed by inverting five conservative tracers (temperature, salinity, phosphate, nitrate, oxygen, and oxygen-18/oxygen-16 ratios). This results in $\mathbf{A}$, a matrix that maps between global surface tracer distributions and the corresponding global interior tracer distribution assumin gthe ocean is at a modern-day circulation. The matrix $\mathbf{A}$ consists of mass fractions from each surface grid cell to every interiro cell. In \citeA[]{Gebbie-Huybers-2012:mean}, this result is extended by incorporating radiocarbon observations, resulting in a discrete time tracer transport oeprator $\mathbf{L}$ which is similar to $\mathbf{A}$, but represents mass fluxes instead of fractions.

\section{Methods}
In this work, we attempt to relate the signals observed at deep sediment core sites to the preceding conditions at the sea surface, accounting for the modification of those signals by ocean circulation, as well as the timescale of their propagation. We decompose the surface into distinct spatial patterns $\mathbf{u}$, and solve for the magnitude of these patterns, for any state variable that affects the value observed at the sediment core. As a physical model equation, we can represent this as a convolution over the relevant state variables $s$ and all lag times $\tau$, 
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:impresp}
y_{t}^c = \sum_s \sum_{\tau} \alpha^s \bm{u}_{t - \tau}^s \cdot \bm{\mathcal{M}}_\tau ^c, 
\end{equation}
where $y_{t}^{c}$ is a property observed at a sediment core $c$ and time $t$, $\alpha^{s}$ is a scaling factor associated with the state variable $s$, $\bm{u}_{t - \tau}^s$ is a vector of all spatial mode magnitudes at time $t-\tau$ and for state variable $s$, and $\bm{\mathcal{M}}_\tau ^c$ is a vector containing the modification of the spatial mode magnitude signal due to ocean circulation at the lagged time $\tau$, and observed at sediment core $c$.

Here, we observe $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ in $\mathbf{y}$, which is a function of temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ in $\mathbf{u}$. As these are largely conservative in the interior ocean, we treat them as passive tracers, and assume linearity. The relationship between the observation vector, $\mathbf{y}$, and the solution vector, $\mathbf{u}$, is characterized by the model matrix, $\mathbf{E}$, and is obtained via the discrete time transport operator $\mathbf{L}$ from \citeA{Gebbie-Huybers-2012:mean}. The usage of $\mathbf{L}$ allows us to constrain the effect of any surface signal on the sediment core sites as a function of time lag from some initial perturbation. This results in the linear inverse problem, 
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:inverse}
\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{Eu}. 
\end{equation}
Individual rows of the model matix $\mathbf{E}$ from Equation (\ref{eq:inverse}) can be identified by making small perturbations to an arbitrary input vector, $\mathbf{u}$, applying Equation (\ref{eq:impresp}), and scaling the output by the perturbation.


In the following sections, we detail how $\mathbf{y}$ (Section \ref{sec:agemodel} and Section \ref{sec:tanddelta}), and the associated covariance matrix $\mathbf{C_{yy}}$ (Section \ref{sec:agemodel}) are formed. As this problem is underdetermined, we construct a prior estimate, $\mathbf{u_0}$, (Section \ref{sec:spatmode}) and its associated statistics, $\mathbf{C_{u_0u_0}}$ (Section \ref{sec:Cuu}). We formulate the model matrix, $\mathbf{E}$, (Section \ref{sec:E}) and present the methodology used to arrive at our best estimate of a solution vector, $\mathbf{\tilde{u}}$, that minimizes misfit to the observation vector, $\mathbf{y}$, and prior estimate, $\mathbf{u_0}$ (Section \ref{sec:solution}).

\subsection{Incorporating Age Model Uncertainty \label{sec:agemodel}}
Age model uncertainty has previously been incorporated into frequency and correlation analysis \cite[]{Khider-Emile-Geay-2022:Pyleoclim}, but has never been accounted for in an inverse framework. We generate mean time series of $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ at evenly-spaced time intervals to populate $\mathbf{y}$ by applying the Bacon age models \cite[]{Bacon, McKay-Emile-Geay-2021:R} from \citeA{Lu-Oppo-2023:Surface}. Each age model realization was linearly interpolated at a common 10-year resolution. Then, for each 10-year timestep, the mean $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ was computed for any interpolated timestep that had more than 100 realizations available, populating $\mathbf{y}$. For a specific sediment core, the associated covariance matrix, $\mathbf{C_{yy}}$, is constructed by computing the covariance between two arbitrary timesteps $t_{1}, t_{2}$,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:covariance}
  \mathbf{C_{yy}}[t_1, t_2] = \frac{\sum_i^M \left(\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_i(t_1) - <\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}(t_1)>
    \right) \left( \delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_i(t_2) - <\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}(t_2)> \right) }{M}, 
\end{equation}
where $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{i}(t_1)$ refers to the interpolated $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ value at $t_{1}$ for realization $i$, $<\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}(t_1)>$ is the mean value across all realizations at timestep $t_1$, and $M$ is the total number of realizations with interpolated values at $t_{1}$ and $t_{2}$. Realizations will have different start and end times due to the Bayesian age modeling process, so there are not necessarily 1000 realizations at every timestep. Finally, a measurement uncertainty of 0.07$\permil$ \cite{Lu-Oppo-2023:Surface} was added as a diagonal element on to the covariance matrix. 

\subsection{$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ as a linear function of temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$}
In the inversion presented here, we solve for the time-evolving surface patterns of temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ that best explain the $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ values observed at the core site. Although solving for the two governing variables is more computationally challenging than solving for a unified effective surface $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ metric (as was done in \citeA[]{Amrhein-Gebbie-2015:Inferring}), temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ are more interpretable and easily compared to common climate model output. To further constrain this underdetermined problem, we use statistics from climate model output to constrain the relative covariance of temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$. 

The measured $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ of a foraminiferal test is dependent upon the ambient temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ at the time of calcification \cite[]{Urey-1947, McCrea-1950:Isotopic}. The $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ is in turn affected by equilibrium fractionation processes such that higher $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ is associated with saltier waters, while lower $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ is associated with fresher waters \cite[]{Emiliani--1955:Pleistocene, Bemis-Spero-1998:Reevaluation}. Ultimately, higher values of $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ are associated with either cooler temperatures and/or more saline waters. \citeA{marchitto} established a linear approximation for $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ as a function of temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ using coretop foraminiferal samples. This relationship takes the form 
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:cibs}
\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}} = \alpha T + \beta + (\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}} - 0.27\permil),
\end{equation}
where $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ is in $\permil$ Pee Dee Belemnite (PDB) and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ is in $\permil$ Standard Mean Ocean Water (SMOW). For \textit{C. wuellerstorfi}, $\alpha = -0.224 \pm 0.002$ $\permil\mathrm{ K}^{-1}$ and $\beta = 3.53 \pm 0.02\permil$. While \textit{C. wuellerstorfi} calcifies at equilibrium, \textit{U. peregrina} is isotopically heavier by 0.47$\permil$ \cite[]{marchitto}. In this work, we follow the recommendation of \citeA{marchitto} to remove the equilibration offset from \textit{U. peregrina} records, and apply Equation (\ref{eq:cibs}). 

\subsection{Quantifying the $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ offset from modern-day conditions \label{sec:tanddelta}}

In order to assemble the observation vector $\mathbf{y}$, the values reported from both \textit{C. wuellerstorfi} and \textit{U. peregrina} records must be put on a common scale. The values used to define the prior, $\mathbf{u_0}$, must also be on the same scale, such that the first guess propagated to the core sites, $\mathbf{Eu_0}$, is on the same scale as the reported values of $\mathbf{y}$. The $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ signal at each core site can be considered to consist of variability around some mean signal. In Figure \ref{fig:offset}, we show the range of the central estimate of $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ from each sediment core. We note a depth structure with decreasing $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ as depth increases, as expected due to the presence of denser, and thus colder and saltier, waters at depth. The total range of CE variability is small compared to the core-to-core difference in the mean $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ for the \textit{U. peregrina} sediment cores (MC28A, MC26A, and MC25A).

In order to fit the mean $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ depth structure in our reconstruction of the prior at the core sites ($\mathbf{Eu_{0}}$), we must have a prior that represents surface conditions that generated the depth structure observed in Figure \ref{fig:offset}. We can use Equation (\ref{eq:cibs}) to estimate effective modern-day $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ at the core sites from the global gridded estimate of $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ and temperature from \citeA[]{gebbie2010}. This product is informed by measurements from the World Ocean Circulation Experiment \cite[]{Gouretski-Koltermann-2004:WOCE} and \citeA[]{LeGrande-Schmidt-2006:Global}, and represents the best estimate of temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ that satisfies a steady-state ocean circulation and minimizes misfit to measurements obtained between 1950-2000 CE.

Here, the uncertainty on the effective $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ is very large (0.21$\permil$), which is mostly due to a 0.2$\permil$ uncertainty on the $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ in \citeA{gebbie2010}. In addition, the central estimate of effective $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ is variably offset from the sediment core records, especially for the \textit{U. peregrina} sediment cores. This may be related to the equilibration offset of \textit{U. peregrina}, which was historically considered to be 0.64$\permil$, \cite[]{Shackleton1974AttainmentOI, Duplessy-Shackleton-1984:Record}, but has recently been challenged to be 0.47$\permil$ by \citeA{marchitto}.

This implies that, if modern-day observations were used to construct the prior, $\mathbf{u_0}$, the inversion would be minimizing misfit to the depth structure, not to the CE signal at each sediment core site. Therefore, due to conflicting values in the equilibration offset of \textit{U. peregrina} \cite[]{Shackleton1974AttainmentOI, marchitto}, as well as uncertainty in realistic modern day estimates of $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ at depth, we choose to work in anomaly space. The decision to work in anomaly space allows us to ensure that we fit the CE signal recorded in each sediment core, instead of the depth structure of $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$, which is difficult to quantitatively interpret. In order to convert our  $\mathbf{y}$ vector into anomaly space, we subtract the mean interpolated values between 1850-1980 CE from each record. We establish the prior that surface oceanic conditions were such that, when waters are propagated to depth, the effective $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ from the ambient waters will be equivalent to the 1850-1980 CE mean value at each sediment core.

\subsection{Computing surface spatial modes \label{sec:spatmode}}
Previous approaches to similar problems have sought to obtain either an individual history at each grid point \cite{Gebbie-2012:Tracer, Amrhein-Gebbie-2015:Inferring} or large, uniformly varying surface regions \cite{gebbie19}. The advantage of the first approach is that regional behavior can be isolated, but challenges related to the high number of degrees of freedom in the problem remain. The second approach reduces the degrees of freedom, but can obscure regional behavior. Here, we propose a middle ground, in which relevant surface spatial modes to the sediment cores are established. By solving for the time-evolving magnitude of these surface spatial modes, we can still resolve regional behavior while minimizing the number of degrees of freedom in the problem. This approach bears similarity to the solution technique of \citeA{Amrhein-Gebbie-2015:Inferring}, in which the singular value decomposition of the model matrix is used to reduce the degrees of freedom in the problem. However, the methodology proposed here explicitly solves for the magnitude of distinct spatial modes, which increases the interpretability of the results.  

The relationship between the water mass composition at each core site and every surface grid cell can be obtained by inverting for the vectorized surface map of mass fractions $\mathbf{s_{c}}$ that best explains the composition of waters at the core site, according to the steady-state TMI matrix, $\mathbf{A}$. This is accomplished by solving 
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{d}_c = \mathbf{A s}_c
\label{eq:swinv}
\end{equation}
for $\mathbf{s}_c$, which is a vectorized surface map of source water contribution as a percentage to some core site $c$. The vector, $\mathbf{d}_c$, is a unit vector containing the location of the sediment core, $c$, interpolated to the TMI grid system. 

Modes characterizing the variability of the surface source water maps can be identified via singular value decomposition. Each vector, $\mathbf{s}_c$, can be transposed and  concatenated into a matrix, $\mathbf{S}$, (with dimensions sediment core, $c$, by TMI surface grid cell, $g$), which we then column-weight by a Gaussian distance matrix, $\mathbf{\mathcal{N}}$, with a radius of 1000 km in order to enforce spatial smoothing. Column-weighting will not affect the general character of the matrix, and only acts to smooth the spatial characteristics of the eigenvectors [NOTE: Jake, you asked me to explain ``why you can multiply by N without changing the solution'' - is this good? I'm a little confused.]. The singular value decomposition of the resultant matrix is taken, resulting in the following,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:svd}
\mathbf{S \mathcal{N}} = \mathbf{U\Sigma} \mathbf{V}^{T},
\end{equation}
which contains orthonormal right singular vectors in the matrix, $\mathbf{V}$, that highlight common regions of importance in terms of source water contribution, while maximizing the orthogonality of the modes. The diagonal matrix $\mathbf{\Sigma}$ contains the singular values, $\sigma_m$, associated with each mode $m$. The left singular vectors contained in $\mathbf{U}$ explain the normalized importance of each surface spatial mode identified in $\mathbf{V}$ to each core site. The right singular values can also be referred to as the empirical orthogonal functions (EOFs), while the left singular values, scaled by the singular values, can be referred to as the principal components (PC) (see Figure S\ref{supp-fig:U}). We can compute the percentage of variance in $\mathbf{S}$ that each mode $m$ explains \cite[]{Hannachi-Jolliffe-2007:Empirical}, $r_m$, through the ratio of the norm of the singular value associated with each mode, $\sigma_m$, to the norm of the matrix $\mathbf{\Sigma}$, 
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:pervar}
r_m = \frac{\sigma_m^2}{\sum_{m} \sigma_{m}^{2}}.
\end{equation}
These spatial modes form a complete basis set for the steady-state problem, but can still be applied to the transient problem, as the only difference is propagation speed, not the spatial pattern of circulation.

Modes are shown in Figure \ref{fig:svdmodes}, where the first mode explains 98.9\% of the variability in source water origination maps for all core sites. Mode 1 characterizes the general surface region that is most important to the sediment core sites, and consists of a single-signed signal with maximum magnitude located directly north of Iceland. This signal is strong across most of the Norwegian and Iceland Seas, and slightly weaker, but still present, in the Irminger Sea. Mode 2, which explains 0.972\% of the variability in source water origination maps for all core sites, is characterized by a single-signed signal in the Nordic Seas, and an anti-phased signal in the Labrador and Irminger Seas. This implies that source water origination maps for each core site tend to have an anti-phased contribution between the Nordic Seas and the Labrador and Irminger Seas (e.g., a sediment core location that has more water originating from the Nordic Seas will have less water originating from the Labrador and Irminger Seas, and vice versa). This relationship is expected from the hydrography of the region, as demonstrated by \citeA{Lu-Oppo-2023:Surface}. 

Although Mode 1 explains almost all of the variability in the surface origination maps, this does not necessarily imply that it is the only surface mode needed to explain the sediment core data. In Figure S\ref{supp-fig:U}, we show the left singular vectors, or unscaled principal components, of the surface source water origination maps, and note that Mode 1 has a consistently-valued negative contribution towards every sediment core site. This implies that we cannot explain any depth-dependent variability using solely Mode 1. In Figure \ref{fig:recon}, we show the sediment core $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ records, and note that there is some depth-dependent behavior, such as that records from sediment cores in intermediate waters (MC28A, MC25A) exhibit less of a positive trend in $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ than sediment cores in deep waters (MC13A, MC14A). In addition, we note some consistent centennial-scale variability in sediment cores MC21A, MC20A, and MC19A. In order to explain these features, which are only present in certain depth ranges, there must be free parameters that have a depth-dependent effect upon misfit to the sediment core records. However, we do note that the features highlighted here are often-times dwarfed by the uncertainty on the sediment core records, implying that they may not need to be perfectly fit in order to explain the data.

\subsection{Expected statistics of surface spatial mode magnitudes \label{sec:Cuu}}
% see `optinv_decompose.jl` for details on this
The adherence of the best estimate, $\mathbf{\tilde{u}}$, to the prior, $\mathbf{u_0}$, is controlled via the prior's associated covariance matrix $\mathbf{C_{u_0u_0}}$. As stated in Section \ref{sec:tanddelta}, we remove the mean value from 1850-1980 CE from each sediment core in our data vector $\mathbf{y}$, and set our first guess equal to $\mathbf{u_0} = \vec{0}$. Therefore, we want to set realistic bounds for the expected variance of these modes in the covariance matrix $\mathbf{C_{u_0u_0}}$. 

The expected statistics of the surface spatial mode magnitudes are determined using SST fields from historical realization \texttt{r11i1p1f1} of the Community Earth System Model, version 2 (CESM2) \cite{cesm2}. At each timestep, the CESM2 SST fields are linearly interpolated to the TMI grid and vectorized, forming a vector $\mathbf{q}_t$ in which $t$ is a time index in the annually-averaged  CESM2 `historical' run (1850-2015). We can then solve for the vector of surface spatial mode magnitudes, $\mathbf{m}_t$, at some time $t$, through 
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:Cuustats}
\mathbf{Vm}_t = \mathbf{q}_t.
\end{equation}

We can then form the vector $\bm{\sigma}^{2}$, which contains the variance of each mode $m$ over time, computed from the vectors $\mathbf{m}_t$. The diagonal elements of $\mathbf{C_{u_{0}u_0}}$ that are associated with temperature can then be populated according to $\bm{\sigma}^{2}$. Our problem does not have independent constraints on temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$, and our solution will have a linear relationship between temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ by virtue of the enforced relationship in Equation (\ref{eq:cibs}). Unlike sea surface temperature, there are no time-varying observational gridded estimates of $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$, and modeled $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ products are still quite novel \cite[]{Brady-Stevenson-2019:Connected}. For this reason, we set the expected variance of the prior's $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ mode magnitudes equal to the calculated variance of temperature in CESM2 for each mode $m$, $\bm{\sigma}^2$, scaled by some factor $\gamma$ to account for the different units and magnitudes.

In order to obtain the scaling factor $\gamma$, we use the isotope-enabled version of the fully-coupled, CESM (iCESM1.2) Last Millennium Ensemble (LME), ensemble member 2 \cite[]{Brady-Stevenson-2019:Connected}. Temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ are observed at a subsurface site ($\sim$1500m) at the grid cell nearest to the sediment core locations. At this location in the model output, temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ have a relationship that is roughly $\gamma = $ 10 K/$\permil$, which is further confirmed through the spatial relationship of temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ in the steady-state, modern-day TMI solution \cite{gebbie2010} (see Supplemental Figures S\ref{supp-fig:td18O}, S\ref{supp-fig:td18O_cesm}). The diagonal elements of $\mathbf{C_{u_0u_0}}$ that are associated with $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ is populated by 
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:corr}
  \frac{\bm{\sigma}_{m}^{2}}{\gamma}.
\end{equation}
The final constraint on the statistics of the prior is the expected correlation between temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$. Despite non-perfect correlation between temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ in reality, we choose to set the correlation coefficient $\rho = 1$. As there are not independent constraints on temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$, but linearity is enforced via the problem definition, the solution will similarly have a linear relationship between the two variables. In order for the solution to have a linear relationship at the value suggested by climate model output analysis ($c = $ 10 K/$\permil$), we must set $\rho=1$. Therefore, the off-diagonal elements of $\mathbf{C_{u_0u_0}}$ associated with the covariance between temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ will be populated by 
\begin{equation}
 \rho \frac{\bm{\sigma}_m^2}{\gamma}. 
\end{equation}
\subsection{Propagating surface spatial modes \label{sec:E}}
Following the approach in \citeA[]{Gebbie-2012:Tracer}, we will propagate each surface spatial mode $\mathbf{v}_m$ through the transient TMI matrix by solving the differential equation 
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:diffeq}
\mathbf{c}_m(t + \Delta t) = \mathbf{Lc}_m(t), 
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{L}$ is the discrete time tracer transport operator and $\mathbf{c}_m$ is the magnitude of the propagated spatial mode $\mathbf{v}_m$ at each TMI grid cell, interior and exterior.

In order to initialize Equation (\ref{eq:diffeq}), we prescribe a spatial mode as the initial condition and use the Dirichlet boundary condition mapping matrix, $\mathbf{B}$, to map this surface condition onto a matrix, as in
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:2}
\mathbf{c}_{m} = \mathbf{Bv}_m.
\end{equation}
We then solve Equation (\ref{eq:diffeq}) for $\mathbf{c}_m(t)$ over some large period of time, such as 1-2000 years at 1 year resolution with a quasi-constant timestep numerical differentiation function (QNDF)  solver \cite[]{doi:10.1137/S1064827594276424}, and observe the result at the core site locations. This is equivalent to computing the step response to a constant-in-time boundary condition, which is much more computationally efficient than computing the response to a time-varying boundary condition. The impulse response, or the response to a surface condition that persists for 1 year, and then disappears, can be computed by taking the time derivative of the step response.

The resultant array is $\bm{\mathcal{M}}$, in which every value will be the propagated spatial mode magnitude, $m$, observed at the sediment core, $c$, site at a particular lagged time, $\tau$, from some arbitrary initial time. We can consider this array to characterize the effect throughout time at any core site from an impulse signal of the form of a surface spatial mode $\mathbf{v}_m$. In Supplemental Figure S\ref{supp-fig:modemags}, we show the impulse response to each surface spatial mode at three core sites between 1190-2129 m depth. Here, Mode 1 has nearly identical evolution at the sediment core sites, which is reasonable considering that it is extremely similar to the mean surface water contribution map, averaged across all sediment core sites. Mode 2 demonstrates that the positive-signed anomaly north of Iceland is more important for deep sediment cores, while the negative-signed anomaly south of Iceland affects the intermediate sediment core sites. This further reinforces our choice to not truncate any modes, as the modes which explain a low percentage of the variance in surface source water origination maps can be used to explain depth-varying behavior at the sediment core sites.

\subsection{Solution method \label{sec:solution}}
We solve Equation (\ref{eq:inverse}) for the time-varying magnitudes of surface patterns, $\mathbf{u}$, by using the Gauss-Markov Theorem. This methodology minimizes uncertainty, as defined according to \cite[]{Wunsch-2006:Discrete}, through the cost function, 
\begin{equation}  
\label{eq:costfunc}
J = \left(\mathbf{y}-\mathbf{\tilde{y}}\right)^T\mathbf{C_{yy}}^{-1}\left(\mathbf{y}-\mathbf{\tilde{y}}\right)+(\mathbf{\tilde{u} - u_0})^T\mathbf{C_{u_{0}u_{0}}}^{-1}(\mathbf{\tilde{u} - u_0}), 
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{\tilde{u}}$ represents values of $\mathbf{u}$ that best minimize $J$ and $\mathbf{u_0}$ is the prior, or first guess, of $\mathbf{\tilde{u}}$. The vector $\mathbf{\tilde{y}}$ is the estimated values at the core sites according to $\mathbf{\tilde{u}}$ (e.g. $\mathbf{\tilde{y}} = \mathbf{E\tilde{u}}$). %Finally, the matrices $\mathbf{C_{yy}}$ and $\mathbf{C_{u_0u_0}}$ are covariance matrices defining the expected statistics of $\mathbf{y}$ and $\mathbf{u_0}$, respectively.

We can solve for the value of $\mathbf{\tilde{u}}$ that minimizes $\frac{d\tilde{J}}{d\mathbf{\tilde{u}}}$, 
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{\tilde{u}} = \mathbf{u_{0}} + \mathbf{C_{u_{0}u_{0}}} \mathbf{E}^T\left(\mathbf{E}\mathbf{C_{u_{0}u_{0}}}\mathbf{E}^T + \mathbf{C_{yy}}\right)^{-1}\left(\mathbf{y}-\mathbf{Eu_0}\right)
\label{eq:twls}.
\end{equation}
The associated covariance can be computed according to
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{C_{\tilde{u}\tilde{u}}} = \mathbf{C_{u_0u_0}}-\mathbf{C_{u_0u_0}}\mathbf{E}^T(\mathbf{E}\mathbf{C_{u_0u_0}}\mathbf{E}^T + \mathbf{C_{yy}}) \mathbf{E}\mathbf{C_{u_0u_0}}. 
    \label{eq:twlsCuu}
    \end{equation}
\subsection{Analysis techniques}
Once we arrive at an estimate, $\mathbf{\tilde{u}}$, we can visually examine the fit of the data by computing the solution reconstruction at the core sites. This is achieved by computing $\mathbf{E\tilde{u}}$, which has an associated covariance matrix 
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:solcov}
\mathbf{C_{\tilde{y}\tilde{y}}} = \mathbf{E C_{\tilde{u}\tilde{u}}E}^T. 
\end{equation}

Output surface maps of temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ throughout time can be constructed by propagating spatial mode magnitudes, at a specific time, $t_u$, and for a specific state variable, $s$, through the $\mathbf{V}$ matrix, 
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:surfrecon}
  \mathbf{\tilde{s}}[t_u, s] = \mathbf{V \tilde{u}}[t_u, s]. 
\end{equation}
The associated covariance matrix will be
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:surfreconunc}
\mathbf{C_{\tilde{s}\tilde{s}}}[t_u, s] = \mathbf{V C_{\tilde{u}\tilde{u}}}[t_u, s]\mathbf{V}^T.
\end{equation}

In summary, we have proposed a methodology that produces the temporal evolution of distinct surface modes of variability that best explain benthic sediment core data. This methodology propagates a full representation of the first and second statistical moements of the observations throughout the problem. For the first time, we simultaneously account for factors such as the age model uncertainty, regional variability, the competing effects of temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ upon $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$, and the modification and lag of surface signals due to ocean circulation. 
\section{Results}

Both solutions exhibit a scaled, time-evolving version of the Mode 1 pattern, as expected from the mode magnitudes. The two solutions exhibit slightly different temporal evolutions, with the \texttt{OPT-11} reconstruction exhibiting cooler-than-industrial conditions in the 1700-1770 CE period, while \texttt{OPT-3} is always warmer than the industrial period. One cause for this discrepancy between the two solutions could be that the 1850-1970 CE mean captures different temporal extents of industrial warming. From visually inspecting Figure \ref{fig:recon}, we know that the \texttt{OPT-11} solution captures more industrial warming than the \texttt{OPT-3} solution, so this may explain why the \texttt{OPT-11} solution has a cooler-than-industrial LIA, while the \texttt{OPT-3} solution does not. 

Our estimates of temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ can be combined back into $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ via Equation (\ref{eq:cibs}), and compared to the planktonic records from the sediment core records \cite{Lu-Oppo-2023:Surface}. Each sediment core has a paired \textit{Globigerina bulloides} and \textit{Globorotalia inflata} record, and a planktonic stack can be computed by following the same procedure as in Section \ref{sec:agemodel} and averaging across all timeseries. Here, we compute our planktonic stack using the sediment cores in the \texttt{OPT-3} solution.

\section{Discussion}
Limitations of this research include the enforced correlation of 10 K/$\permil$ between temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$. In the future, independent constraints on temperature could be used to remove this constraint. In addition, constraining the expected variance of spatial mode magnitudes from CESM output implies that we are seeking out solutions that have similar ocean variability to the simulated historical (1850-2005) ocean. Therefore, our solutions should be interpreted as possible solutions for surface histories of $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ and temperature with similar variability and correlations to the modern-day ocean. This does not exclude the possibility of a reality in which these relationships are different, but suggests that this dataset does not necessitate changes in these relationships.

An important limitation of the solutions presented here is that the Mode 1 surface spatial pattern presented is not the only surface spatial pattern that can fit the sediment core records. In Supplemental Section S\ref{supp-sec:sols}, we demonstrate that a uniform global surface pattern can fit the observations as well as the \texttt{OPT-3} solution over the LIA. However, this does not mean that any arbitrary pattern is able to fit the sediment core data, as we demonstrate by attempting, and failing, to fit the sediment core records with only Mode 2. Our sediment core signals are best fit by a surface pattern that has a similar propagation signal at the sediment core sites, which not all surface patterns will have, due to the timescales of ocean circulation. Taken on their own, there is no way to infer from the benthic sediment core records alone if the globally-uniform pattern, or the Mode 1 pattern, is more likely to have been the correct solution. However, as exhibited in Figure \ref{fig:ocean2kcomp}, the spatial pattern of Mode 1 is replicated by the rate of cooling in planktonic Mg/Ca records, as previously discussed by \citeA[]{Sicre-Hall-2011:Sea}. This pattern has previously been attributed to a migration of the polar front and weakening of the subpolar gyre, according to the mechanism suggested by \citeA[]{Hatun-Sando-2005:Influence}. 

An important result of this research is the first estimate of LIA surface cooling inferred from benthic sediment core records. Our reconstruction suggests a fairly high magnitude cooling during the last millennium (-0.71 $\pm$ 0.28 K/kyr). \citeA[]{Cunningham-Austin-2013:Reconstruction} reconstructed SST for the northeast North Atlantic using a composite plus regression approach to integrate planktonic foraminiferal records. LIA cooling was estimated to be 0.2 $^{\circ}$C/kyr, and was suggested to appear after 1250 CE and persisted until the early 20th Century. As previously stated, \citeA{ocean2k} report a global mean SST cooling of 0.42 $^{\circ}$C/kyr, which lies almost exactly on the -1$\sigma$ line of our reconstruction. In contrast, terrestrial cooling in the surrounding regions has been estimated to be 0.47 $^{\circ}$C/kyr in the Arctic, 0.09$^{\circ}$C/kyr in Europe, and 0.34 $^{\circ}$C/kyr in North America \cite[]{Consortium--2013:Continental}. This contrast between a strong ocean cooling, and weaker land cooling is unexpected when compared to prior observations of the terrestrial amplification of climate change \cite[]{Byrne-OGorman-2018:Trends, Seltzer-Blard-2023:Terrestrial}. 

In Figure \ref{fig:meants}, we noted considerable disagreement between the LMR and our reconstructions. The LMR is an offline data assimilation product, which uses CMIP models and twentieth-century reanalysis products to construct a prior and constrains it through the PAGES2k database \cite[]{Hakim-Emile-Geay-2016:last}. Given that PAGES2k constructs a weaker land cooling than our reconstructions and Ocean2k, and that models may be expected to amplify terrestrial temperature change relative to ocean temperature change, it is perhaps unsurprising that the LMR exhibits a very subtle LIA cooling over the North Atlantic. This result suggests that either i) the amount of terrestrial cooling may be underestimated in PAGES2k or ii) the products used to inform the prior in the LMR may not reflect the relevant ocean dynamics associated with the LIA. Given that evidence exists for persistent model biases related to North Atlantic dynamics in CMIP-class models \cite[]{Heuze2017}, the second hypothesis may hold more weight. 

One physical explanation for the higher magnitude cooling observed in the North Atlantic relative to most land masses could be the amplification of Arctic temperature change. \citeA{Miller-Alley-2010:Arctic} used a variety of summer paleoclimate syntheses to demonstrate Arctic amplification of 3-4 times the global mean temperature during the Holocene Thermal Maximum, Last Glacial Maximum, and mid-Pliocene. However, Arctic amplification has never been identified in Common Era records, although it has been shown to exist in coupled climate models \cite{ottobliesner16}. Although amplified Arctic warming of up to four times the magnitude of the global mean signal has been identified in observations since 1980 \cite[]{Park-Kim-2019:Mid}, Arctic amplification has not been clearly identifiable during the entirety of the instrumental record \cite[]{England-Eisenman-2021:Arctic}. This has been attributed to the cancellation of greenhouse gas warming by anthropogenic aerosol release, as well as sensitivity to the phase of internal variability during periods of forcing \cite[]{England-Eisenman-2021:Arctic}.

\section{Conclusion}
Here, we have demonstrated a novel methodology of inferring surface climate conditions from benthic sediment core records via an ocean circulation model, while accounting for the lag in transit time from the surface to depth, age model uncertainty, and the unequal contributions of temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ towards $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$. In addition, dimension reduction via a source water contribution decomposition was utilized to increase the computational tractability of the problem. The effects of temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ were separated via established experimental relations and correlations from climate model output. This methodology resulted in the first 1000-year reconstruction of sea surface temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ from benthic sediment core records.

We generated two possible solutions, the \texttt{OPT-3} solution, which spanned the onset of the LIA, and the \texttt{OPT-11} solution, which captured only the peak of the LIA and the subsequent recovery toward industrial conditions. However, the \texttt{OPT-3} solution was only constrained by three sediment cores, while the \texttt{OPT-11} solution was constrained by 11. We found that the data could be well fit in both solutions by using the primary mode of surface water origination at the sediment core sites, which was characterized by a strong anomaly north of Iceland. This spatial pattern was also observed to occur in the magnitude of LIA cooling observed by Ocean2k sediment cores in the region of interest. Both solutions, when recombined into effective $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$, were shown to agree with the independent planktonic records from the sediment cores.

In this work, we present a new estimate of North Atlantic surface cooling, which is within the range of the estimate from the Ocean2k compilation \cite[]{ocean2k}. We demonstrate that the spatial pattern of amplified LIA cooling in the Nordic Seas, as previously identified in a planktonic Mg/Ca record from sediment core MD99-2275, is a plausible solution for a pattern of surface cooling that could explain the signals recorded in the benthic $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ compilation of \citeA{Lu-Oppo-2023:Surface}. The identified North Atlantic SST cooling is outside of the range of that represented by the Last Millennium Reanalysis \cite[]{Hakim-Emile-Geay-2016:last, Tardif2019}, suggesting that the usage of a forward model to map largely-terrestrial proxies to global climate variables may be fairly inaccurate in the North Atlantic.

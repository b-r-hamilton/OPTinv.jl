\section{Quantifying the $\mathrm{\delta^{18}O_{calcite}}$ offset from modern-day conditions \label{sec:offset}}
In Section \ref{main-sec:y}, we discuss the relationship between the sediment core records and effective modern-day $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$, i.e., the modern-day calibration problem. In our problem set-up, we seek to be able to define a prior estimate, $\mathbf{\hat{u_{0}}}$ (Section \ref{main-sec:solution}), that, when propagated through the model matrix $\mathbf{E}$, results in values that are on the same scale as the observational vector $\mathbf{y}$. 

 We use Equation (\ref{main-eq:cibs}) to estimate effective modern-day $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ at the core sites from the global gridded estimate of $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ and temperature from \citeA{gebbie2010}. This product is informed by measurements from the World Ocean Circulation Experiment \cite{Gouretski-Koltermann-2004:WOCE}, as well as gridded estimates of $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ \cite{LeGrande-Schmidt-2006:Global}, and represents the best estimate of temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ that satisfies a steady-state ocean circulation and minimizes misfit to measurements obtained between 1950-2000 CE.

The uncertainty on the effective $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ is large (0.21$\permil$) compared to the observational range of CE variability ($\sim$0.1$\permil$), which is mostly due to a 0.2$\permil$ uncertainty on the $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ in this location in \citeA{gebbie2010}. Most notably, the central estimate of effective $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ is variably offset from the observed value at each sediment core site, especially for the \textit{U. peregrina} sediment cores (Figure \ref{main-fig:offset}). For MC28A, the effective $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ is offset from the observed $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ by 0.2$\permil$, which is almost double the range of CE variability and similar to the uncertainty on the effective $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$. The other \textit{U. peregrina} sediment cores (MC26A and MC25A) also exhibit offsets from the central estimate of effective $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ that exceed the range of CE variability. This may be related to the inter-species equilibration offset of \textit{U. peregrina}, which was historically considered to be 0.64$\permil$, \cite{Shackleton1974AttainmentOI,Duplessy-Shackleton-1984:Record}, but has recently been challenged to be 0.47$\permil$ by \citeA{marchitto}. Here, we have applied the 0.47$\permil$ offset to the the \textit{U. peregrina} records, but if the true equilibration offset were closer to 0.64$\permil$, then the observed $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ values would be closer to the central estimate of effective $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$.

As stated in the Section \ref{main-sec:y}, in order to prioritize reconstructing LIA magnitude with limited effect from time-invariant errors, we convert all quantities into anomaly from 1850-1980 CE, as in
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:6}
y = y_{\mathrm{observed}} - \bar{y}_{\mathrm{1850-1980 CE}}
\end{equation}
By converting the observational vector into anomaly space, and setting the prior $\mathbf{\hat{u_{0}}}$ equal to 0, the optimized mode magnitudes $\mathbf{\hat{\tilde{u}}}$ will intrinsically be anomalies from 1850-1980 CE. This prioritizes estimating the LIA magnitude with minimal errors or biases due to the modern-day calibration. 

\section{Tracer transport model \label{sec:ttm}}
In Supplemental Section \ref{sec:Gprime}, we first seek to relate signals at the interior sediment core locations to each surface grid cell, forming \ref{main-eq:modeleq}. Then, in Supplemental Section \ref{sec:Mprime}, we seek to relate signals at the interior sediment core locations to arbitrary surface spatial modes, forming Equation \ref{main-eq:modeleqfin}. 
\subsection{Formulation of $\mathbf{G\prime}$ \label{sec:Gprime}}
As stated in the text, the matrix $\mathbf{G\prime}$ relates interior signals to surface signals that occur at any relevant prior time. We form $\mathbf{G\prime}$ by individually modeling an impulse signal (i.e., a pulsed signal that turns on at one timestep, and turns off at the next time step) at every surface grid cell, and observing its propagated arrival at the interior locations of interest. Then, any interior observation at time $t$ can be related to surface signals at any preceding time, at any surface grid cell. For computational ease, we model a surface step-signal, and then take the first derivative of the interior response signal to form the boundary propagator signal. 

Each element of $\mathbf{G}'$ is associated with the relationship between a specific sediment core record $c$ at a specific observation time $t_{y}$, and a constant-in-time signal, $\mathbf{q}_{i}$, at a preceding state vector time $t_{u}$. The signal, $\mathbf{q}_{i}$ takes the form of a global surface ocean vector that has null values in all cells, besides grid cell $i$ which will be set equal to 1. We will define the relationship between these quantities at a range of lagged times, $\tau = t_{u} - t_{y}$. Following Equations (\ref{main-eq:diffeqspec}) and (\ref{main-eq:yeff}), we will then compute the step response function $g_{\mathrm{step}}$, 
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:mtau}
g_{\mathrm{step}}(t+\Delta t) = \mathbf{o}_{c}^{T}\left(\mathbf{Lc}(t) + \mathbf{Bq}_{i}\right), 
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{o}_{c}$ is the interpolation vector that effectively ``observes'' a three-dimensional output vector $\mathbf{c}$ at the sediment core sites, and all other variables are the same as in Equation (\ref{main-eq:diffeqspec}). We initialize the interior tracer concentration as $\mathbf{c} = \vec{0}$ at the initial time $t_{0} = 0$, and recursively compute the value of $g_{\mathrm{step}}$ at all times of interest $t_{0 }+n \Delta t $. The step response function represents the interior response at some time $t_{0}+n\Delta t$ to a surface signal that ``turns on'' at an initial time $t_0 = 0$. We recursively compute the value of $g_{\mathrm{step}}$ at a range of lagged times $\bm{\tau}$, which are 
\begin{align}
  \label{eq:tau}
  \bm{\tau} = 
\begin{bmatrix}
\tau_{0}\\
\tau_{1}\\
\vdots \\
\tau_{n}
\end{bmatrix}
    =
 \begin{bmatrix}
 t_{0} \\
t_{0}+ \Delta t \\
   \vdots\\
t_{0} + N_{\tau u} \Delta t
 \end{bmatrix},
\end{align}
resulting in the vector of values $g_{\mathrm{step}}(\bm{\tau})$. Here, $N_{\tau u}$ is the total number of timesteps we wish to compute the step response function.

We can compute the boundary propagator function, $g(\bm{\tau})$, which represents the interior response to an instantaneous surface signal, by using a finite time centered difference scheme to compute the first derivative in time of $g_{\mathrm{step}}(\bm{\tau})$. The boundary propagator function can be considered as  a surface signal that ``turns on'' at the initial timestep $t_{0}$, and then ``turns off'' at the next time step. We compute a set of boundary propagator functions for all sediment core sites $c$, all boundary conditions $\mathbf{q}_{i}$, and at all relevant lagged times $\tau$. Each element of the range (columns) of matrix $\mathbf{G}'$ is defined by a sediment core site $c$ and an observation time $t_{y}$.  Each element of the domain (rows) of $\mathbf{G}'$ is defined by a state vector time $t_{u}$ and a boundary condition $\mathbf{q}_{i}$. Each element of $\mathbf{G}'$ is therefore defined by a lagged time $\tau$, and will be populated by the appropriate boundary propagator function from $g(\bm{\tau})$.
\subsection{Formulation of $\mathbf{M\prime}$ \label{sec:Mprime}}

In order to connect Equations (\ref{main-eq:modeleq}) and Equation (\ref{main-eq:modeleqfin}), we first consolidate Equation (\ref{main-eq:surfbc}) by establishing a vector $\mathbf{u}$ which can be multiplied by the matrix $\mathbf{V}_{\mathrm{arb}}$, 
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:2}
\mathbf{q}(t_{u}) = \mathbf{V}_{\mathrm{arb}}\mathbf{u}(t_{u}). 
\end{equation}
The vector $\mathbf{u}(t_{u})$ consists of all $N$ mode magnitude values, $u_{i}$, at a particular state vector time, $t_{u}$. Similarly, the matrix $\mathbf{V}_{\mathrm{arb}}$ consists of all $N$ spatial surface modes concatenated into a $N \times 11,113$ matrix. We can then expand Equation (\ref{main-eq:modeleq}) by substituting for each $\mathbf{q_{T}}$ and $\mathbf{q_{s}}$, 
\begin{align}
\label{eq:1}
\begin{bmatrix}
\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{eff}}(t_{f})\\
\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{eff}}(t_{f-1})\\
\vdots \\
\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{eff}}(t_{f-Y})
\end{bmatrix}                         
  = \mathbf{G}' \left( \alpha
  \begin{bmatrix}
\mathbf{V}_{\mathrm{arb}}\mathbf{u_{T}}(t_{f}) \\
\mathbf{V}_{\mathrm{arb}}\mathbf{u_{T}}(t_{f-1})\\
\vdots \\
\mathbf{V}_{\mathrm{arb}}\mathbf{u_{T}}(t_{f-Q})
  \end{bmatrix} +
  \begin{bmatrix}
\mathbf{V}_{\mathrm{arb}}\mathbf{u_{s}}(t_{f})\\
\mathbf{V}_{\mathrm{arb}}\mathbf{u_{s}}(t_{f-1})\\
\vdots \\
\mathbf{V}_{\mathrm{arb}}\mathbf{u_{s}}(t_{f-Q})
  \end{bmatrix}  
  \right). 
\end{align}
Since the matrix $\mathbf{V}_{\mathrm{arb}}$ is not time-varying, it can be factored out in the form of a block-diagonal matrix $\mathbf{V}_{\mathrm{block}}$, 
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:3}
\mathbf{\hat{y}}_{\mathrm{eff}} = \mathbf{G'V}_{\mathrm{block}} (\alpha \mathbf{\hat{u}_{T}} + \mathbf{\hat{u}_{s}}). 
\end{equation}
We refer to the product $\mathbf{G'V}_{\mathrm{block}}$ as $\mathbf{M'}$, the spatial mode boundary propagator, which leads us to Equation (\ref{main-eq:modeleqfin}). However, computing and storing both $\mathbf{G}'$ and $\mathbf{V}_{\mathrm{arb}}$ is computationally expensive, and can be circumvented by directly computing $\mathbf{M'}$. This can be accomplished by setting $\mathbf{q}_{i}$ equivalent to $\mathbf{v}_{i}$ in Supplemental Equation (\ref{eq:mtau}), taking the finite time central difference to compute the boundary propagator, and assembling all boundary propagator functions into the matrix $\mathbf{M'}$. 

\section{Surface spatial modes \label{sec:spatmode}}
In order to relate water mass fluxes at the sediment core sites to the surface, we apply an ocean circulation model to generate surface water origination maps. For an estimate of modern-day, steady-state circulation, we use the results of \citeA{gebbie2010}, in which a box inversion technique is used to compute the pathways connecting interior ocean grid boxes to the surface. The global ocean is decomposed into a $2^{\circ} \times 2^{\circ}$ grid with 33 vertical levels, and the pathways that connect each interior grid box to the surface are computed by inverting five tracers (temperature, salinity, phosphate, nitrate, oxygen, and oxygen-18/oxygen-16 ratios). This results in $\mathbf{A}$, a matrix that maps between global surface tracer distributions, $\mathbf{s}$, and the corresponding global interior tracer distribution, $\mathbf{c}$, assuming the ocean is at a steady-state modern-day circulation, as
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ss}
\mathbf{s} = \mathbf{Ac}. 
\end{equation}
The matrix $\mathbf{A}$ consists of the mass fractions of waters propagated from any surface grid cell to any interior cell.
% JG: at first glance, confused why this isn't c = A s

The relationship between the water mass composition at each core site and every surface grid cell can be obtained by inverting Supplemental Equation (\ref{eq:ss}) for $\mathbf{s_{c}}$, which is a vectorized surface map of source water contribution to some core site $c$. This is accomplished by setting $\mathbf{c}$ equivalent to a unit vector containing the location of the sediment core interpolated to the grid system used in the Total Matrix Intercomparison (TMI) method \cite{gebbie2010}. Therefore, $\mathbf{s_c}$ will be a normalized surface map, in which every grid cell will be assigned a value corresponding to the fraction of water at the core site location that is contributed by the specified surface grid cell. 

Modes characterizing the variability of the surface source water maps can be identified via singular value decomposition. Each vector, $\mathbf{s_{c}}$, can be transposed and concatenated into a matrix, $\mathbf{S}$, (with dimensions sediment core by TMI surface grid cell), which we then column-weight by a Gaussian distance matrix, $\bm{\mathcal{N}}$, with a radius of 1000 km in order to enforce spatial smoothing and provide information about the distance between gridcells. The radius of the Gaussian distance matrix is chosen to reflect the spatial scale of large-scale variability in the surface ocean. Column-weighting will not affect the general character of the matrix, and only acts to spatially smooth the singular vectors. The singular value decomposition of the resultant matrix, $\bm{S \mathcal{N}}$ is taken, resulting in the following decomposition,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:svd}
\bm{S \mathcal{N}} = \mathbf{U\Sigma} \mathbf{V}^{T},
\end{equation}
which contains orthonormal right singular vectors in the matrix, $\mathbf{V}$, that highlight common regions of importance in terms of source water contribution, while maximizing the orthogonality of the modes. The diagonal matrix, $\mathbf{\Sigma}$, contains the singular values, $\sigma_m$, associated with each mode $m$. The left singular vectors contained in $\mathbf{U}$ explain the normalized importance of each surface spatial mode to each core site. The right singular values can also be referred to as the empirical orthogonal functions (EOFs), while the left singular values, scaled by the singular values, can be referred to as the principal components (PC) (see Supplemental Figure \ref{fig:U}). We can compute the percentage of variance in $\bm{S\mathcal{N}}$ that each mode $i$ explains \cite{Hannachi-Jolliffe-2007:Empirical}, $r_i$, through the ratio of the norm of the singular value associated with each mode, $\sigma_i$, to the norm of the matrix $\mathbf{\Sigma}$, 
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:pervar}
r_i = \frac{\sigma_i^2}{\sum_{j} \sigma_{j}^{2}}.
\end{equation}
These spatial modes form a complete basis set for the steady-state problem, but can still be applied to the transient problem, as the only difference is propagation speed, not circulation structure.

\section{Expected statistics of surface spatial mode magnitudes \label{sec:Cuu}}
\subsection{Temporal variance of temperature \label{sec:tempvar}}
In order to set a realistic variance for the prior, $\mathbf{\hat{u_0}}$, we use SST fields from historical realization \texttt{r11i1p1f1} of the Community Earth System Model (CESM), version 2 \cite{cesm2}. At each model timestep, the decadally-averaged CESM2 SST fields are linearly interpolated to the TMI grid and vectorized, forming a vector $\mathbf{s}_\mathrm{model}(t)$ in which $t$ is a time index in the annually-averaged  CESM2 `historical' run (1850-2015). We then solve for the vector of surface spatial mode magnitudes, $\mathbf{u}_\mathrm{model}$, at some time $t$, by inverting
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:Cuustats}
\mathbf{Vu}_\mathrm{model}(t) = \mathbf{s}_\mathrm{model}(t).
\end{equation}
As an example, we show the $u_{\mathrm{model}}(t)$ timeseries associated with Mode 1 in Supplemental Figure \ref{fig:umodel}A. The Mode 1 $u_{\mathrm{model}}(t)$ timeseries has similar properties to the CESM2 mean temperature in a North Atlantic box centered on the region where Mode 1 has the most power (0$^{\circ}$-20$^{\circ}$E, 60$^{\circ}$N-80$^{\circ}$N), as expected. We compute the standard deviation of $\mathbf{u_{\mathrm{model}}}$ for each mode, at all times, which we refer to as the vector $\bm{\sigma}$ (Supplemental Figure \ref{fig:umodel}B). For the more coherent modes, such as Modes 1 and 2, $\bm{\sigma}$ has higher standard deviation. The standard deviation drops off for higher-order modes, which are less coherent in space. We assume that the variance of signals during the CE was less than 4 times 1850-1980 CE variability. Therefore, the diagonal elements of $\mathbf{C_{u_{0}u_0}}$ that are associated with temperature are populated according to $(4\sigma_{i})^{2}$.

\subsection{Temporal variance of $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ and temperature:$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ regression \label{sec:oxygenvar}}

The diagonal elements of $\mathbf{C_{u_{0}u_0}}$ that are associated with $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ are populated according to $\bm{\sigma}$, scaled by the temperature:$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ regression value, $\gamma$. The final constraint on the statistics of the prior is the expected correlation between temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$. This is controlled via the off-diagonal elements of $\mathbf{C_{u_0u_0}}$ associated with the covariance between temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$. For a given mode $m$, off-diagonal values will be populated by  $\rho (4\sigma_m)^2\gamma^{-1}$, where $\rho$ is the correlation coefficient, and controls how tightly the estimate will be held to the regression value $\gamma$. Despite non-perfect correlation between temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ in reality, we choose to enforce perfect correlation by setting the correlation coefficient, $\rho$, equal to one. As there are no independent constraints on temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$, but linearity is enforced via the problem definition, the estimate will have a linear relationship between the two variables regardless of the correlation coefficient, $\rho$. In order for the estimate to have a linear relationship between temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ at the regression value, $\gamma$, we must set the correlation coefficient, $\rho$, to 1. If the correlation coefficient were set to a lower value, the relationship between temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ would still be perfectly correlated, but have a linear relationship different from that defined by the prescribed regression value $\gamma$.

In \ref{main-sec:corr}, we used output from a Last Millennium Ensemble (LME) realization of iCESM1.2 to estimate the temperature:$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ regression value. Temperature and salinity timeseries have similar temporal characteristics between equivalent realizations of CESM1.1 and iCESM1.2 (Supplemental Figure \ref{fig:cesmts}). Both temperature and salinity, in each simulation, exhibit a characteristic ``red noise'' signal, with high frequency variability superimposed on lower frequency variability. The exact signals vary in each simulation, with the most unifying feature being a subtle CE cooling. In contrast, the iCESM1.2 $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ timeseries exhibits step-function characteristics, with high frequency variability and some extreme values superimposed. We would expect the salinity and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ signals to have fairly similar characteristics, as they are affected by similar processes.

In Section \ref{main-sec:gammasens}, we suggested that an alternative approach to calculating a regression value, $\gamma$, would be to define the relationship based upon the correlation between temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ in a depth transect at this location, which we obtain from the output of \citeA{gebbie2010}. In Supplemental Figure \ref{fig:td18O}, we find a relationship of $T = 15.51S$, which is much higher than the relationship identified in coupled climate model output in \ref{main-sec:corr} ($T = 5.34S$). The $S = 2.257\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}$ relationship identified in Supplemental Figure \ref{fig:td18O} is closer to the global mixing line of $S = 2\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ than the value identified in the coupled climate model output. This is expected, as we are now looking at a spatial relationship, not a temporal relationship.

\subsection{Proportion of sediment core record variance explainable by prior statistics of each mode \label{sec:Cy0y0}}
After constructing our prior covariance matrix, $\mathbf{C_{u_{0}u_{0}}}$, we can assess the amount of variance in each sediment core record that is able to be explained by each surface spatial mode. Similarly to Equation \ref{main-eq:solcov}, we propagate the prior covariance matrix through the model matrix, arriving at the reconstructed sediment core site prior covariance matrix, $\mathbf{C_{y_{0}y_{0}}}$. This matrix describes the statistics of the propagated prior, as in 
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:y0cov}
\mathbf{C_{y_{0}y_{0}}} = \mathbf{E C_{u_{0}u_{0}}E}^T. 
\end{equation}
We can assess the proportion of variance explainable by an individual mode $i$ by zeroing out the components of $\mathbf{C_{u_{0}u_{0}}}$ associated with all other modes. This covariance matrix represents the statistics of the possible solution space of reconstructed sediment core records, illustrating what the prior is able to reconstruct. Ideally, this will encompass a range of reasonable reconstructions in the context of the observational and age modelling uncertainties, while also being informed by the model statistics discussed in Supplemental Sections \ref{sec:oxygenvar} and \ref{sec:tempvar}. 
\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centerline{\includegraphics[width = 0.75\textwidth]{/home/brynn/Code/OPTinv.jl/plots/Cy0y0 allvold.png}}
\caption[]{\label{fig:Cy0y0} Standard deviation of sediment core records reconstructed according to the prior, explained by each surface spatial mode. MC26A (1190 m) is shown in orange, MC22A (1535 m) in blue, and MC13A (2129 m) in purple.}
\end{figure}

In Figure \ref{fig:Cy0y0}, we see that Mode 1 can explain the highest amount of variance in each sediment core record, near 0.07$\permil$ (1 s.e.), which is near the observational uncertainty of the sediment core records. Modes 2-6 are able to explain lower amounts of variance, however, as stated in Section \ref{main-sec:spatmode}, the higher-order modes are interpreted as modes that will be able to help us explain depth-dependent variability in the sediment core records. An example of depth-dependent variability in the sediment core records is the centennial-scale oscillations observed in sediment cores MC21A, MC20A, and MC19A (Figure \ref{main-fig:recon}). These oscillations have a magnitude of less than 0.05$\permil$, within the range of the standard deviation associated with the projection of Modes 2-6 onto the sediment core sites. Therefore, our choices in prior statistics are appropriate for the problem at hand. By summing across all sediment cores, and using Equation \ref{eq:pervar}, we arrive at the transient problem percentage variance displayed in Figure \ref{main-fig:svdmodes}.

\section{Uniqueness of estimates: \texttt{OPT-3-GLOBAL} and \texttt{OPT-3-MODE2} \label{sec:smest}}
  The procedure for generating both \texttt{OPT-3-GLOBAL} and \texttt{OPT-3-MODE2} is identical to that described for \texttt{OPT-3}. The impulse response due to a global surface signal (Supplemental Figure \ref{fig:impulse}) bears similarity to the impulse response due to Mode 1 (Supplemental Figure \ref{fig:modemags}), in that the signal is fairly similar when observed at the three sediment cores of interest. The response at the sediment core sites due to a Mode 2 impulse response consists of a negative signal at MC26A and MC22A, with larger amplitude on MC26A than MC22A, and a positive signal at MC13A. Mode 2 is characterized by a negative anomaly south of Iceland, and a positive anomaly north of Iceland (Figure \ref{main-fig:svdmodes}). Waters from the Nordic Seas north of Iceland are expected to experience deep convection, and will affect the deep sediment core site MC13A at 2129 m more than the shallower sites MC26A (1190 m) and MC22A (1535 m), hence why the MC13A experiences a negative signal due to Mode 2, while MC26A and MC22A experience a positive signal.

\section{Sensitivity of estimate to temperature:$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ regression \label{sec:gammasens}}
In Supplemental Figure \ref{fig:gamma}A, we estimate mean high-latitude North Atlantic temperature change as a function of the regression value, $\gamma$. We find that the mean high-latitude North Atlantic temperature change required to explain a change in $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ of 0.08$\permil$ (an approximation of the mean change of the sediment core records) is largely stable for values of $\gamma$ greater than 10 K$\permil^{-1}$. Both the value used in the main text ($\gamma = 14.93$ K$\permil^{-1}$) and the alternative value from Supplemental Section \ref{sec:oxygenvar} ($\gamma = 35.01$ K$\permil^{-1}$) are in the stable region of this equation. However, as $\gamma$ decreases towards the critical point of $\gamma = \alpha^{-1} = 4.45 $ K$\permil^{-1}$, the mean high-latitude North Atlantic temperature rapidly decreases to negative infinity. For values of $\gamma$ greater less than, but near the critical value, the mean high latitude North Atlantic temperature change is positive. 

In \ref{main-sec:corr} and Supplemental Section \ref{sec:oxygenvar}, we estimate the temperature:$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ regression value, $\gamma$, by estimating the salinity:$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ regression value, which we will refer to as $\gamma_{s}$, and estimating the temperature:salinity regression value, which we will refer to as $\gamma_{t}$, and multiplying these two value together, such that,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:4}
T = \gamma_{t}\gamma_{s} \delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}. 
\end{equation}
In Supplemental Figure \ref{fig:gamma}B, we examine the sensitivity of high-latitude North Atlantic temperature change to both $\gamma_{s}$ and $\gamma_{t}$. In the estimates provided in the main text, we use a regression value of $\gamma = 14.93 $ K$\permil^{-1}$, which is obtained from isotope-enabled coupled climate model output in \ref{main-sec:corr}. This value is formed from the subordinate regression values $\gamma_{t}$ = 5.34 K (g kg$^{-1}$)$^{-1}$ and $\gamma_{s}$ = 2.796 g kg$^{-1}\permil^{-1}$. In Supplemental Section \ref{sec:oxygenvar}, we obtain subordinate regression values of $\gamma_{t} = 15.51 $ K(g kg$^{-1}$)$^{-1}$ and $\gamma_{s}$ = 2.257 g kg$^{-1}\permil^{-1}$ using temperature, salinity, and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ values at the sediment core site locations in the output of \citeA{gebbie2010}. As in Supplemental Figure \ref{fig:gamma}A, we find that the mean high-latitude North Atlantic temperature change is largely insensitive to either of the two estimates of $\gamma$. In order for the estimate of mean high-latitude North Atlantic temperature change to significantly differ from the magnitude presented here, it would require a significant decrease in the $\gamma$ regression value.

\section{Timestep choice and estimate sensitivity \label{sec:timestep}}
Although the average radiocarbon age uncertainty is 40 years for these records, the age model is constrained by other independent information, such as the sediment accumulation rate and its autocorrelation, estimated through $^{210}$Pb activity. The median spacing of the age models from \citeA{Lu-Oppo-2023:Surface} is between 1.1 and 17.9 years (Supplemental Figure \ref{fig:medianagemodelres}). In geoChronR, measurements within a resolution are binned into temporal intervals larger than the median resolution, with the interval chosen to intentionally degrade the median age resolution \cite{McKay-Emile-Geay-2021:R}. For the \citeA{Lu-Oppo-2023:Surface}, this is a 5- to 10-year bin \cite{Lu-Oppo-2023:Surface}. We choose a timestep of 10 years to reflect the larger of these two bin sizes.

\section{Mathematical notation}
In this manuscript, the tilde (e.g. $\tilde{x}$) is used to denote a quantity that has either been estimated via the Gauss-Markov Theorem, or derived from a quantity that has been estimated via the Gauss-Markov Theorem. The hat (e.g. $\hat{\mathbf{x}}$) is used to denote a vector quantity that has been concatenated in time. The prime notation (e.g. $\mathbf{X}'$) is used to denote some type of propagator matrix. 

In Supplemental Table \ref{var}, all variables used in this manuscript are defined, for the ease of the reader. All vectors, and matrices have units following the approach of \citeA{Hart-1995:Multidimension}, in which the domain and range are assumed to each be associated with an appropriately sized vector of units. Most unit vectors in this work are uniform. Here, a matrix associated with the dimensionality ($\permil \times$ K) would be assumed to have a range defined by a uniform vector with units $\permil$, and a domain defined by a uniform vector with units of K. For the quantities that have non-uniform range or domains, the unit vector is defined in a footnote. In this table, the $\times$ symbol is always assumed to separate the range and domain, while the $\cdot$ symbol multiplies two quantities within either the range or domain. In addition, the dimensionality $N_{ty}$ refers to the number of timesteps in the observations, $N_{i}$ to the number of TMI interior grid cells, $N_{s}$ to the number of TMI surface grid cells, $N_{c}$ to the number of cores, $N_{tu}$ to the number of timesteps in the state vector $\mathbf{u}$, and $N$ to the number of modes.

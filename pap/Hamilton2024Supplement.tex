\section{Temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ Relationship}
An important parameter in the inversion presented in this work is the enforced relationship between temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$. Intuitively, we expect $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ and salinity to be strongly correlated, as they are both controlled by the same processes (evaporation, precepitation). We expect some correlation between temperature and salinity, as cooler waters tend to be fresher and warmer waters tend to be saltier. Therefore, we also expect some correlation between temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$. We explore the relationship between temperature, salinity, $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ first in the global, gridded, surface output from \citeA{gebbie2010}. These results confirm that salinity and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ are most strongly correlated (R$^{2}$ = 0.756), while temperature and salinity (R$^{2}$ = 0.224), and temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}$ (R$^{2}$ = 0.511) are less related, but still exhibit some correlation. Here, we note a roughly linear (R$^2$ = 0.51) relationship between temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$.
\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centerline{\includegraphics[width = \textwidth]{/home/brynn/Code/OPTinv.jl/plots/T_v_d18O.png}}
\caption[]{Relationships between temperature and salinity \textbf{A}, salinity and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\textrm{seawater}}$ \textbf{B}, and temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ for global surface values in the TMI output \cite{gebbie2010}. \label{fig:td18O}}
\end{figure}

However, our problem is constrained by the relationship between temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ temporally, not spatially. Time-evolving, paired measurements of $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ and temperature are difficult to obtain for the instrumental era, and we are specifically interested in the covariance of these variables on longer timescales. For this reason, we explore the relationship between these variables in a coupled model. We use output from a single realization (002) of the fully-forced, isotope-enabled Community Earth System (iCESM1.2) Last Millenium Ensemble (LME) \cite[]{Brady-Stevenson-2019:Connected}. The results from this, while also not strictly linearly, reflect similar relationships to the relationship found in \cite[]{Gebbie-Huybers-2010:Total}. 

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centerline{\includegraphics[width = \textwidth]{/home/brynn/Code/OPT2k.jl/plots/cesm_scatter.png}}
\caption[]{\label{fig:td18O_cesm}Relationships between temperature and salinity \textbf{A}, salinity and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\textrm{seawater}}$ \textbf{B}, and temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ from decadally-averaged timeseries from an interior grid cell near the sediment core locations (51.6 $^{\circ}$N, 21.9$^{\circ}$W, 1500m) in realization 002 of the iCESM1.2 LME. }
\end{figure}

\section{Solution Comparison to Instrumental Record}
We can compute time-varying surface maps of temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ via Equation (\ref{main-eq:surfrecon}), which is shown in Figure \ref{main-fig:sst}. Although observational time series on relevant timescales are rare, we average the SST map in a Labrador Sea box, and compare it to a comparable region in the Hadley Centre Sea Ice and Sea Surface Temperature dataset (HadISST) \cite[]{Rayner-Parker-2003:Global}, as well as a digitized long-term Labrador Sea Water observational dataset \cite[]{LSG--1998:Labrador} in Supplemental Figure S\ref{fig:labcomp}. An approximation of salinity can be arrived at from our $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ reconstruction by using the ratio between salinity and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ changes of 0.5255 \cite{marchitto}, which can be compared to the long-term salinity record from \citeA{LSG--1998:Labrador}.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centerline{\includegraphics[width = \textwidth]{/home/brynn/Code/OPTinv.jl/plots/labcompallvold.png}}
\caption[]{\label{fig:labcomp} \textbf{(A)} Reconstructed Labrador Sea (60$^{\circ}$W-30$^{\circ}$E, 55$^{\circ}$N-64$^{\circ}$N) temperature and \textbf{(B)} salinity (computed as $0.5255 \times \delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$) over the instrumental period (1850-1970) is shown for \texttt{OPT-3} (blue) and \texttt{OPT-11} (red) solutions. Solid line shows digitized Labrador Sea record \cite{LSG--1998:Labrador}. Dashed line shows winter HadISST \cite{Rayner-Parker-2003:Global} Labrador Sea spatial mean (60$^{\circ}$W-30$^{\circ}$E, 55$^{\circ}$N-64$^{\circ}$N). All records are shown as an anomaly from 1970.}
\end{figure}

We expect some smoothing in our solution reconstruction, as a result of using benthic records which do not experience near-surface variability nor seasonal variations. This is evident in both the temperature and salinity panels in Supplemental Figure S\ref{fig:labcomp}. The magnitude of changes in reconstructed temperature and salinity are within the range of decadal-scale variability for the sparse instrumental records in our region of interest. 

\section{Additional Solutions \label{sec:sols}}
As a test of the significance of the Mode 1 pattern, we provide an estimate in which, instead of using 11 distinct spatial modes, we use one globally uniform mode. In addition, we compute our solution using solely Mode 2, to illustrate if any North Atlantic pattern would be equally as skillful at fitting the sediment core observations. We refer to these two new solutions as \texttt{OPT-3-GLOBAL} and \texttt{OPT-3-MODE2}. The procedure is identical to that described in the main text, and we set the expected variance of the globally uniform mode for \texttt{OPT-3-GLOBAL} and Mode 2 for \texttt{OPT-3-MODE2} in the prior equal to the variance of Mode 1 in the \texttt{OPT-3} and \texttt{OPT-11} solutions. The cost functions are presented in Supplemental Table S\ref{tab:cost}.

\begin{table}
\caption{Cost function values for each solution \label{tab:cost}}
\centering
\begin{tabular}{l | c | c | c }
\hline
 Inversion  & $\mathbf{n}^T\mathbf{C_{nn}}^{-1}\mathbf{n}$ & $\mathbf{u_0}^T\mathbf{C_{u_0u_0}}^{-1}\mathbf{u_0}$ & $\mathbf{n}^T\mathbf{C_{nn}}^{-1}\mathbf{n} + \mathbf{u_0}^T\mathbf{C_{u_0u_0}}^{-1}\mathbf{u_0}$\\
\hline
  \texttt{OPT-3-GLOBAL}  & 13.1 & 7.17 &  20.3  \\
  \texttt{OPT-3-MODE2}  &  89.9 & 3.98 & 93.9  \\
\hline
%\multicolumn{2}{l}{$^{a}$Footnote text here.}
\end{tabular}
\end{table}
In Supplemental Table S\ref{tab:cost}, the \texttt{OPT-3-GLOBAL} solution has a very similar value to \texttt{OPT-3} for the portion of the cost function associated with the observational misfit (6.28 for \texttt{OPT-3}, 13.1 for \texttt{OPT-3-GLOBAL}, with an expected value of 572). \texttt{OPT-3} and \texttt{OPT-3-GLOBAL} solutions represent very similar fits to the sediment core data, which may suggest to the reader that any arbitrary surface pattern could fit the data within error. However, the portion of the cost function associated with observational misfit is much higher for \texttt{OPT-3-MODE2}, although still less than the expected value. This implies that the, although a uniform global surface pattern can fit the sediment core data as well as the SVD-derived Mode 1, arbitrary North Atlantic surface patterns are not guaranteed to fit the sediment core signals. 

To further examine this finding, we recreate Figure \ref{main-fig:recon}, but for \texttt{OPT-3-GLOBAL} and \texttt{OPT-11-GLOBAL}. In Supplemental Figure S\ref{fig:recon}, the \texttt{OPT-11-GLOBAL} solution has a fairly similar core site reconstruction to the \texttt{OPT-11} solution. However, the \texttt{OPT-3-GLOBAL} reconstruction has a much worse fit to sediment cores MC22A and MC13A than the \texttt{OPT-3} solution, implying that the spatial pattern of Mode 1 is important for capturing the sediment core data. This can be further investigated by contrasting the response of a globally-uniform impulse versus a Mode 2 impulse, which is shown in Figure \ref{fig:impulse}
\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centerline{\includegraphics[width = \textwidth]{/home/brynn/Code/OPTinv.jl/plots/reconsolglobal.png}}
\caption[]{\label{fig:recon} Reconstruction at core sites for \texttt{OPT-11-GLOBAL} estimate (red line and shading) and \texttt{OPT-3-GLOBAL} estimate (blue line and shading). Black line shows sediment core record. Note inverted $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ axis.}
\end{figure}

The impulse response due to a global surface signal bears similarity to the impulse response due to Mode 1 (see Supplemental Figure S\ref{fig:modemags}), in that the signal is fairly similar when observed at the three sediment cores of interest. The response at the sediment core sites due to a Mode 2 impulse response consists of a negative signal at MC26A and MC22A, with larger amplitude on MC26A than MC22A, and a positive signal at MC13A. Mode 2 is characterized by a negative anomaly south of Iceland, and a positive anomaly north of Iceland (Figure \ref{main-fig:svdmodes}). Waters from the Nordic Seas north of Iceland are expected to experience deep convection, and will affect the deep sediment core site MC13A at 2129 m more than the shallower sites MC26A (1190 m) and MC22A (1535 m), hence why the MC13A experiences a negative signal due to Mode 2, while MC26A and MC22A experience a positive signal. 
\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centerline{\includegraphics[width = 0.8\textwidth]{/home/brynn/Code/OPTinv.jl/plots/Mode2.png}}
\caption[]{\label{fig:impulse} Impulse response due to a global surface signal (\textbf{A}) and a Mode 2 surface signal (\textbf{B}). MC26A (1190 m) is shown in orange, MC22A (1535 m) in blue, and MC13A (2129 m) in purple.}
\end{figure}

\section{Quantifying the $\mathrm{\delta^{18}O_{calcite}}$ offset from modern-day conditions \label{sec:offset}}
In Section \ref{main-sec:solution}, we introduce the concept of the prior, $\mathbf{\hat{u_{0}}}$. The values used to define the prior must be put on a common scale to the problem, such that the prior propagated to the core sites, $\mathbf{E\hat{u_0}}$, is on the same scale as observational vector, $\mathbf{\hat{y}}$. There is a depth structure in the observations with $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ increasing as depth increases, as expected due to the presence of denser, and thus colder and saltier, waters at depth (Figure \ref{main-fig:offset}). In order to fit the $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ depth structure in the reconstructed prior, the prior must represent surface conditions that generate the observed depth structure when propagated to depth. We use Equation (\ref{main-eq:cibs}) to estimate effective modern-day $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ at the core sites from the global gridded estimate of $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ and temperature from \citeA[]{gebbie2010}. This product is informed by measurements from the World Ocean Circulation Experiment \cite[]{Gouretski-Koltermann-2004:WOCE}, as well as gridded estimates of $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ \cite[]{LeGrande-Schmidt-2006:Global}, and represents the best estimate of temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ that satisfies a steady-state ocean circulation and minimizes misfit to measurements obtained between 1950-2000 CE.

The uncertainty on the effective $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ is large (0.21$\permil$) compared to the observational range of CE variability ($\sim$0.1$\permil$), which is mostly due to a 0.2$\permil$ uncertainty on the $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ in this location in \citeA{gebbie2010}. Most notably, the central estimate of effective $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ is variably offset from the observed value at each sediment core site, especially for the \textit{U. peregrina} sediment cores (Figure \ref{main-fig:offset}). For MC28A, the effective $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ is offset from the observed $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ by 0.2$\permil$, which is almost double the range of CE variability and similar to the uncertainty on the effective $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$. The other \textit{U. peregrina} sediment cores (MC26A and MC25A) also exhibit offsets from the central estimate of effective $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ that exceed the range of CE variability. This may be related to the equilibration offset of \textit{U. peregrina}, which was historically considered to be 0.64$\permil$, \cite[]{Shackleton1974AttainmentOI, Duplessy-Shackleton-1984:Record}, but has recently been challenged to be 0.47$\permil$ by \citeA{marchitto}. Here, we have applied the 0.47$\permil$ offset to the the \textit{U. peregrina} records, but if the true equilibration offset were closer to 0.64$\permil$, then the observed $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ values would be closer to the central estimate of effective $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$.

\section{Tracer transport model \label{sec:ttm}}
Each element of $\mathbf{G}'$ is associated with the relationship between a specific sediment core record $c$ at a specific time $t_{y}$, and a constant-in-time signal, $\mathbf{q}_{i}$, at a preceding time $t_{u}$. We will define the relationship between these quantities at a range of lagged times, $\tau$. We assume that the signal, $\mathbf{q}_{i}$ takes the form of a global surface ocean vector that has null values in all cells, besides grid cell $i$ which will be set equal to 1. For computational ease, we will assume $\mathbf{q}_{i}$ to be constant-in-time. Following Equations (\ref{main-eq:diffeqspec}) and (\ref{main-eq:yeff}), we will then compute the step response function $g_{\mathrm{step}}$, 
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:mtau}
g_{\mathrm{step}}(t+\Delta t) = \mathbf{o}_{c}^{T}\left(\mathbf{Lc}(t) + \mathbf{Bq}_{i}\right), 
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{o}_{c}$ is the interpolation vector that effectively ``observes'' a three-dimensional output vector $\mathbf{c}$ at the sediment core sites, and all other variables are the same as in Equation (\ref{main-eq:diffeqspec}). We initialize the interior tracer concentration as $\mathbf{c} = \vec{0}$ at the initial time $t_{0} = 0$, and recursively compute the value of $g_{\mathrm{step}}$ at all times of interest $t_{0 }+n \Delta t $. The step response function represents the interior response at some time $t+\Delta t$ to a surface signal that ``turns on'' at time $t = 0$. We recursively compute the value of $g_{\mathrm{step}}$ at a range of lagged times $\bm{\tau}$, which are 
\begin{align}
  \label{eq:tau}
  \bm{\tau} = 
\begin{bmatrix}
\tau_{0}\\
\tau_{1}\\
\vdots \\
\tau_{n}
\end{bmatrix}
    =
 \begin{bmatrix}
 t_{0} \\
t_{0}+ \Delta t \\
   \vdots\\
t_{0} + N_{tu} \Delta t
 \end{bmatrix},
\end{align}
resulting in the vector of values $g_{\mathrm{step}}(\bm{\tau})$.

We can compute the boundary propagator function, $g(\bm{\tau})$, which represents the interior response to an instantaneous surface signal, by using a finite time centered difference scheme to compute the first derivative in time of $g_{\mathrm{step}}(\bm{\tau})$. The boundary propagator function can be considered as  a surface signal that ``turns on'' for one timestep, and then ``turns off''. We compute a set of boundary propagator functions for all sediment core sites $c$, all boundary conditions $\mathbf{q}_{i}$, and at all relevant lagged times $\tau$. Each element of the range (columns) of matrix $\mathbf{G}'$ is defined by a sediment core site $c$ and an observation time $t_{y}$.  Each element of the domain (rows) of $\mathbf{G}'$ is defined by a state vector time $t_{u}$ and a boundary condition $\mathbf{q}_{i}$. For each element of $\mathbf{G}'$ is therefore defined by a lagged time $\tau = t_{u} - t_{y}$, and will be populated by the appropriate boundary propagator function from $g(\bm{\tau})$. 

In order to connect Equations (\ref{main-eq:modeleq}) and Equation (\ref{main-eq:modeleqfin}), we first consolidate Equation (\ref{main-eq:surfbc}) by establishing a vector $\mathbf{u}$ which can be multiplied by the matrix $\mathbf{V}_{\mathrm{arb}}$, 
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:2}
\mathbf{q}(t) = \mathbf{V}_{\mathrm{arb}}\mathbf{u}(t). 
\end{equation}
The vector $\mathbf{u}(t)$ consists of all $N$ $u_{i}$ values at a particular time. Similarly, the matrix $\mathbf{V}_{\mathrm{arb}}$ consists of all $N$ spatial surface modes concatenated into a $N \times 11,113$ matrix. We can then expand Equation (\ref{main-eq:modeleq}) by substituting for each $\mathbf{q_{T}}$ and $\mathbf{q_{s}}$, 
\begin{align}
\label{eq:1}
\begin{bmatrix}
\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{eff}}(t = 1980)\\
\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{eff}}(t = 1970)\\
\vdots \\
\mathbf{y}_{\mathrm{eff}}(t = 800)
\end{bmatrix}                         
  = \mathbf{G}' \left( \alpha
  \begin{bmatrix}
\mathbf{V}\mathbf{u_{T}}(t = 1980) \\
\mathbf{V}\mathbf{u_{T}}(t = 1970)\\
\vdots \\
\mathbf{V}\mathbf{u_{T}}(t = 500)
  \end{bmatrix} +
  \begin{bmatrix}
\mathbf{V}\mathbf{u_{s}}(t = 1980)\\
\mathbf{V}\mathbf{u_{s}}(t = 1970)\\
\vdots \\
\mathbf{V}\mathbf{u_{s}}(t = 500)
  \end{bmatrix}  
  \right). 
\end{align}
Since the matrix $\mathbf{V}$ is not time-varying, it can be factored out in the form of a block-diagonal matrix $\mathbf{V}_{\mathrm{block}}$, 
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:3}
\mathbf{\hat{y}}_{\mathrm{eff}} = \mathbf{G'V}_{\mathrm{block}} (\alpha \mathbf{\hat{u}_{T}} + \mathbf{\hat{u}_{s}}). 
\end{equation}
We refer to the product $\mathbf{G'V}_{\mathrm{block}}$ as $\mathbf{M'}$, the spatial mode boundary propagator, which leads us to Equation (\ref{main-eq:modeleqfin}). However, computing and storing both $\mathbf{G}'$ and $\mathbf{V}$ is computationally expensive, and can be circumvented by directly computing $\mathbf{M'}$. This can be accomplished by setting $\mathbf{q}_{i}$ equivalent to $\mathbf{v}_{i}$ in Supplemental Equation (\ref{eq:mtau}), taking the finite time central difference to compute the boundary propagator, and assembling all boundary propagator functions into the matrix $\mathbf{M'}$. 

\section{Surface spatial modes \label{sec:spatmode}}
In order to relate water mass fluxes at the sediment core sites to the surface, we apply an ocean circulation model to generate surface water origination maps. For an estimate of modern-day, steady-state circulation, we use the results of \citeA{gebbie2010}, in which a box inversion technique is used to compute the pathways connecting interior ocean grid boxes to the surface. The global ocean is decomposed into a $2^{\circ} \times 2^{\circ}$ grid with 33 vertical levels, and the pathways that connect each interior grid box to the surface are computed by inverting five semi-conservative tracers (temperature, salinity, phosphate, nitrate, oxygen, and oxygen-18/oxygen-16 ratios). This results in $\mathbf{A}$, a matrix that maps between global surface tracer distributions and the corresponding global interior tracer distribution assuming the ocean is at a modern-day circulation, as
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ss}
\mathbf{s} = \mathbf{Ac},
\end{equation}
in which $\mathbf{s}$ is a vector containing tracer values at all surface model grid points, and $\mathbf{c}$ is a vector containing the propagated tracer concentrations at all interior model grid points given a steady-state circulation. The matrix $\mathbf{A}$ consists of the mass fractions of waters propagated from any surface grid cell to any interior cell.
The relationship between the water mass composition at each core site and every surface grid cell, can be obtained by inverting Equation (\ref{eq:ss}) for $\mathbf{s_{c}}$, which is a vectorized surface map of source water contribution to some core site $c$. This is accomplished by setting $\mathbf{c}$ equivalent to a unit vector containing the location of the sediment core, $c$, interpolated to the grid system used in the Total Matrix Intercomparison (TMI) method \cite{gebbie2010}. Therefore, $\mathbf{s_c}$ will be a normalized surface map, in which every grid cell will be assigned a value corresponding to the fraction of water at the core site location that is contributed by the specified surface grid cell. 

Modes characterizing the variability of the surface source water maps can be identified via singular value decomposition. Each vector, $\mathbf{s_{c}}$, can be transposed and concatenated into a matrix, $\mathbf{S}$, (with dimensions sediment core, $c$, by TMI surface grid cell), which we then column-weight by a Gaussian distance matrix, $\bm{\mathcal{N}}$, with a radius of 1000 km (chosen to reflect the spatial scale of large-scale variability in the surface ocean) in order to enforce spatial smoothing. Column-weighting will not affect the general character of the matrix, and only acts to spatially smooth the singular vectors. The singular value decomposition of the resultant matrix is taken, resulting in the following decomposition,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:svd}
\bm{S \mathcal{N}} = \mathbf{U\Sigma} \mathbf{V}^{T},
\end{equation}
which contains orthonormal right singular vectors in the matrix, $\mathbf{V}$, that highlight common regions of importance in terms of source water contribution, while maximizing the orthogonality of the modes. The diagonal matrix $\mathbf{\Sigma}$ contains the singular values, $\sigma_m$, associated with each mode $m$. The left singular vectors contained in $\mathbf{U}$ explain the normalized importance of each surface spatial mode identified in $\mathbf{V}$ to each core site. The right singular values can also be referred to as the empirical orthogonal functions (EOFs), while the left singular values, scaled by the singular values, can be referred to as the principal components (PC) (see Figure S\ref{fig:U}). We can compute the percentage of variance in $\bm{S\mathcal{N}}$ that each mode $i$ explains \cite[]{Hannachi-Jolliffe-2007:Empirical}, $r_i$, through the ratio of the norm of the singular value associated with each mode, $\sigma_i$, to the norm of the matrix $\mathbf{\Sigma}$, 
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:pervar}
r_i = \frac{\sigma_i^2}{\sum_{i} \sigma_{i}^{2}}.
\end{equation}
These spatial modes form a complete basis set for the steady-state problem, but can still be applied to the transient problem, as the only difference is propagation speed, not the spatial pattern of circulation.

\section{Expected statistics of surface spatial mode magnitudes \label{sec:Cuu}}
\subsection{Temporal variance of temperature \label{sec:tempvar}}
In order to set a realistic variance for the prior, $\mathbf{u_0}$, we use SST fields from historical realization \texttt{r11i1p1f1} of the Community Earth System Model (CESM), version 2 \cite{cesm2}. At each model timestep, the decadally-averaged CESM2 SST fields are linearly interpolated to the TMI grid and vectorized, forming a vector $\mathbf{s}_\mathrm{model}(t)$ in which $t$ is a time index in the annually-averaged  CESM2 `historical' run (1850-2015). We then solve for the vector of surface spatial mode magnitudes, $\mathbf{u}_\mathrm{model}$, at some time $t$, by inverting
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:Cuustats}
\mathbf{Vu}_\mathrm{model}(t) = \mathbf{s}_\mathrm{model}(t).
\end{equation}
As an example, we show the $u_{\mathrm{model}}(t)$ timeseries associated with Mode 1 in Supplemental Figure \ref{fig:umodel}A. The Mode 1 $u_{\mathrm{model}}(t)$ timeseries has similar properties to the CESM2 mean temperature in a North Atlantic box centered on the region where Mode 1 has the most power (0$^{\circ}$-20$^{\circ}$E, 60$^{\circ}$N-80$^{\circ}$N). We compute the standard deviation of $\mathbf{u_{\mathrm{model}}}$ for each mode, at all times, which we refer to as the vector $\bm{\sigma}$ (Supplemental Figure \ref{fig:umodel}B). For the more coherent modes, such as Modes 1 and 2, $\bm{\sigma}$ has higher standard deviation, and drops off as the modes become less coherent. We assume that the variance of signals during the CE was less than 4 times 1850-1980 variability. Therefore, the diagonal elements of $\mathbf{C_{u_{0}u_0}}$ that are associated with temperature are populated according to $(4\sigma_{i})^{2}$.

\subsection{Temporal variance of $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ and temperature:$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ correlation \label{sec:oxygenvar}}

The diagonal elements of $\mathbf{C_{u_{0}u_0}}$ that are associated with $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ are populated according to $\bm{\sigma}$, scaled by the temperature:$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ correlation value. The final constraint on the statistics of the prior is the expected correlation between temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$. This is controlled via the off-diagonal elements of $\mathbf{C_{u_0u_0}}$ associated with the covariance between temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$. For a given mode $m$, off-diagonal values will be populated by  $\rho \sigma_m^2\gamma^{-1}$, where $\rho$ is the correlation coefficient, and controls how tightly the estimate will be held to the correlation value $\gamma$. Despite non-perfect correlation between temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ in reality, we choose to enforce perfect correlation by setting the correlation coefficient, $\rho$, equal to one. As there are not independent constraints on temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$, but linearity is enforced via the problem definition, the solution will similarly have a linear relationship between the two variables regardless of the correlation coefficient, $\rho$. In order for the estimate to have a linear relationship between temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ at the correlation constant value, $\gamma$, we must set the correlation coefficient, $\rho$, to 1. If the correlation coefficient were set to a lower value, the relationship between temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ would still be perfectly correlated, but have a linear relationship different from that defined by the prescribed value $\gamma$.

In \ref{main-sec:corr}, we used output from a Last Millennium Ensemble (LME) realization of iCESM1.2 to estimate the temperature:$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ correlation value. Temperature and salinity timeseries have similar temporal characteristics between equivalent realizations of CESM1.1 and iCESM1.2 (Supplemental Figure \ref{fig:cesmts}). Both temperature and salinity, in each simulation, exhibit a characteristic ``red noise'' signal, with high frequency variability superimposed on lower frequency variability. The exact signals vary in each simulation, with the most unifying feature being a subtle CE cooling. In contrast, the iCESM1.2 $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ timeseries appears to be more of a step-function signal, with high frequency variability and some extreme values superimposed. We would expect the salinity and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ signals to have fairly similar characteristics, as they are affected by similar processes. 
\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centerline{\includegraphics[width = \textwidth]{cesm_timeseries.png}}
\caption[]{\label{fig:cesmts} Timeseries from an interior grid cell near the sediment core locations (51.6 $^{\circ}$N, 21.9$^{\circ}$W, 1500m) of (\textbf{A, B}) temperature and salinity in a full-forcing ensemble member 6 of the CESM1.1 Last Millennium Ensemble \cite[]{ottobliesner16}, and (\textbf{C, D, E}) temperature, salinity, and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ in realization 002 of the iCESM1.2 Last Millennium Ensemble \cite[]{Brady-Stevenson-2019:Connected}. In all figures, the gray line shows the monthly output from each model, and the black line shows the output with a 5-year binned averaging scheme applied for all output after 950 CE. In Figure \ref{main-fig:td18O}, only data after the year 950 is used, in order to minimize any effects from model spin-up.}

In Section \ref{main-sec:gammasens}, we suggested that an alternative approach to calculating a correlation constant, $\gamma$, would be to define the relationship based upon the correlation between temperature and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ in a depth transect at this location, which we obtain from the output of \citeA{gebbie2010}. In Supplemental Figure \ref{fig:td18O}, we find a relationship of $T = 16S$, which is much higher than the relationship identified in coupled climate model output in \ref{main-sec:corr}, which we is near the density-compensation ratio ($T = 5.3S$). The $S = 2.3\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}$ relationship identified in Supplemental Figure \ref{fig:td18O} is closer to the global mixing line of $S = 2\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ than the value identified in the coupled climate model output. This is expected, as we are now looking at a spatial relationship, not a temporal relationship.

\section{Uniqueness of estimates: \texttt{OPT-3-GLOBAL} and \texttt{OPT-3-MODE2} \label{sec:smest}}
  The procedure for generating both \texttt{OPT-3-GLOBAL} and \texttt{OPT-3-MODE2} is identical to that described for \texttt{OPT-3}. The impulse response due to a global surface signal bears similarity to the impulse response due to Mode 1 (see Supplemental Figure \ref{fig:modemags}), in that the signal is fairly similar when observed at the three sediment cores of interest. The response at the sediment core sites due to a Mode 2 impulse response consists of a negative signal at MC26A and MC22A, with larger amplitude on MC26A than MC22A, and a positive signal at MC13A. Mode 2 is characterized by a negative anomaly south of Iceland, and a positive anomaly north of Iceland (Figure \ref{main-fig:svdmodes}). Waters from the Nordic Seas north of Iceland are expected to experience deep convection, and will affect the deep sediment core site MC13A at 2129 m more than the shallower sites MC26A (1190 m) and MC22A (1535 m), hence why the MC13A experiences a negative signal due to Mode 2, while MC26A and MC22A experience a positive signal.

\section{Sensitivity of estimate to temperature:$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ correlation \label{sec:gammasens}}
In Supplemental Figure \ref{fig:gamma}A, we estimate mean high-latitude North Atlantic temperature change as a function of the correlation constant $\gamma$. We find that the mean high-latitude North Atlantic temperature change required to explain a change in $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{calcite}}$ of 0.08$\permil$ (an approximation of the mean change of the sediment core records) is largely stable for values of $\gamma$ greater than 10 K$\permil^{-1}$. Both the value used in the main text ($\gamma = 14.8$ K$\permil^{-1}$) and the alternative value from \ref{main-sec:corr} ($\gamma = 36.8$ K$\permil^{-1}$) are in the stable region of this equation. However, as $\gamma$ decreases towards the critical point of $\gamma = \alpha^{-1} = 4.45 $ K$\permil^{-1}$, the mean high-latitude North Atlantic temperature rapidly decreases to negative infinity. For values of $\gamma$ greater less than, but near the critical value, the mean high latitude North Atlantic temperature change is positive. 

In \ref{main-sec:corr} and Supplemental Section \ref{sec:oxygenvar}, we estimate the temperature:$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ correlation constant, $\gamma$, by estimating the salinity:$\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ correlation constant, which we will refer to as $\gamma_{s}$, and estimating the temperature:salinity correlation constant, which we will refer to as $\gamma_{t}$, and multiplying these two constants together, such that,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:4}
T = \gamma_{t}\gamma_{s} \delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}. 
\end{equation}
In Supplemental Figure \ref{fig:gamma}B, we examine the sensitivity of high-latitude North Atlantic temperature change to both $\gamma_{s}$ and $\gamma_{t}$. In the estimates provided in the main text, we use a correlation constant of $\gamma = 14.8 $K$\permil^{-1}$, which is obtained from isotope-enabled coupled climate model output in \ref{main-sec:corr}. This value is formed from the subordinate correlation constants $\gamma_{t}$ = 5.3 K (g kg$^{-1}$)$^{-1}$ and $\gamma_{s}$ = 2.3 g kg$^{-1}\permil^{-1}$. In Supplemental Section \ref{sec:oxygenvar}, we obtain subordinate correlation constants of $\gamma_{t} = 16 $ K (g kg$^{-1}$)$^{-1}$ and $\gamma_{s}$ = 2.3 g kg$^{-1}\permil^{-1}$ using temperature, salinity, and $\delta^{18}\mathrm{O}_{\mathrm{seawater}}$ values at the sediment core site locations in the output of \citeA{gebbie2010}. As in Supplemental Figure \ref{fig:gamma}A, we find that the mean high-latitude North Atlantic temperature change is largely insensitive to either of the two estimates of $\gamma$. In order for the estimate of mean high-latitude North Atlantic temperature change to significantly differ from the magnitude presented here, it would require a significant decrease in the $\gamma$ correlation. The estimate is more sensitive to variations in $\gamma_{s}$ than $\gamma_{t}$.

\section{Mathematical notation}
In this manuscript, the tilde (e.g. $\tilde{x}$) is used to denote a quantity that has either been estimated via the Gauss-Markov Theorem, or derived from a quantity that has been estimated via the Gauss-Markov Theorem. The hat (e.g. $\hat{\mathbf{x}}$) is used to denote a vector quantity that has been concatenated in time. The prime notation (e.g. $\mathbf{X}'$) is used to denote some type of propagator matrix. 

In Supplemental Table \ref{var}, all variables used in this manuscript are defined, for the ease of the reader. All vectors, and matrices have units following the approach of \citeA[]{Hart-1995:Multidimension}, in which the domain and range are assumed to each be associated with an appropriately sized vector of units. Most unit vectors in this work are uniform. Here, a matrix with an entry of ($\permil \times$ K) would be assumed to have a range defined by a uniform vector with units $\permil$, and a domain defined by a uniform vector with units of K. For the quantities that have non-uniform range or domains, the unit vector is defined in a footnote. In this table, the $\times$ symbol is always assumed to separate the range and domain, while the $\cdot$ symbol multiplies two quantities within either the range or domain. In addition, the dimensionality $N_{ty}$ refers to the number of timesteps in the observations, $N_{i}$ to the number of TMI interior grid cells, $N_{s}$ to the number of TMI surface grid cells, $N_{c}$ to the number of cores, $N_{tu}$ to the number of timesteps in the state vector $\mathbf{u}$, and $N$ to the number of modes.
